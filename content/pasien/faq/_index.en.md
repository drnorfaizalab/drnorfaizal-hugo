## 1. About Brain Tumours

**Q: What is a brain tumour, and what types are there?**

A brain tumour is an abnormal growth of cells within the brain or its surrounding structures. Tumours may be **primary** — originating in the brain itself — or **secondary (metastatic)** — having spread to the brain from a cancer elsewhere in the body, such as the lung, breast, or colon.

Primary brain tumours include gliomas (which include glioblastoma, astrocytoma, and oligodendroglioma), meningiomas (arising from the membranes covering the brain), pituitary tumours, acoustic neuromas (vestibular schwannomas), ependymomas, and many others. Each type has a different biology, behaviour, and treatment approach. Not all brain tumours are malignant — many, such as most meningiomas, are benign in nature.

---

**Q: What are the symptoms of a brain tumour?**

Symptoms depend heavily on the location, size, and growth rate of the tumour. Common presentations include:

- Persistent or worsening headaches, often worse in the morning or when lying down
- New-onset seizures in an adult with no prior history of epilepsy
- Progressive weakness or numbness in an arm or leg
- Difficulty with speech, language comprehension, or finding words
- Changes in vision — double vision, loss of peripheral vision, or blurred vision
- Balance problems or difficulty walking
- Personality or behavioural changes
- Cognitive decline or memory problems

It is important to note that many of these symptoms are also caused by benign conditions. The presence of one or more of these symptoms does not mean you have a brain tumour — but persistent or progressive symptoms warrant evaluation.

---

**Q: How is a brain tumour diagnosed?**

Diagnosis begins with a clinical assessment by a neurologist or neurosurgeon, followed by neuroimaging. **MRI of the brain with contrast** is the gold standard investigation for suspected brain tumours. CT scanning is often performed in emergency settings. In some cases, advanced imaging techniques such as MR spectroscopy, perfusion MRI, or PET-CT are used to provide additional information.

A definitive tissue diagnosis requires **biopsy or surgical resection** with pathological examination of the tumour tissue. Modern neurooncology also involves **molecular profiling** — testing tumour tissue for specific genetic markers (such as IDH mutation, MGMT promoter methylation, and 1p/19q codeletion) that influence prognosis and treatment planning.

---

**Q: Are brain tumours hereditary?**

The vast majority of brain tumours are not directly inherited. Most arise from sporadic genetic mutations that occur during a person's lifetime. However, certain hereditary conditions — such as neurofibromatosis type 1 and 2, von Hippel-Lindau disease, Li-Fraumeni syndrome, and tuberous sclerosis — are associated with an increased risk of developing specific types of brain tumours. If there is a strong family history of brain tumours or associated conditions, genetic counselling may be appropriate.

---

**Q: What is a glioblastoma, and is it treatable?**

Glioblastoma (GBM, or glioblastoma multiforme) is the most aggressive form of primary brain tumour, classified as a Grade 4 glioma. It is unfortunately the most common malignant primary brain tumour in adults.

Treatment typically involves a combination of surgery (to remove as much of the tumour as safely possible), radiotherapy, and chemotherapy with temozolomide — a regimen established by the landmark Stupp protocol. While glioblastoma is not currently curable, treatment significantly extends survival and preserves quality of life. Ongoing research into immunotherapy, targeted molecular therapies, and tumour treating fields (TTF) offers hope for continued progress.

Dr Nor Faizal has specific expertise in the surgical management of glioblastoma and coordinates closely with radiation and medical oncology teams to deliver an integrated treatment approach.

---

**Q: Can a brain tumour cause epilepsy?**

Yes. New-onset seizures in an adult — particularly in someone with no prior history of epilepsy — are one of the most common presentations of a brain tumour, particularly low-grade gliomas. The tumour disrupts normal electrical activity in surrounding brain tissue, triggering abnormal discharges. Not all brain tumours cause seizures, and not all new-onset adult epilepsy is caused by a brain tumour — but any new seizure in an adult warrants urgent neuroimaging to exclude a structural cause.

## 2. About Surgery

**Q: When is surgery recommended for a brain tumour?**

The decision to operate depends on many factors: the type, size, and location of the tumour; the patient's neurological function; the patient's age and general health; and the likely diagnosis based on imaging characteristics. Surgery is typically recommended when:

- The tumour is causing significant mass effect or neurological deficit
- A tissue diagnosis is required (biopsy)
- The tumour can be safely and significantly debulked to improve prognosis and facilitate subsequent treatment
- The tumour is causing hydrocephalus (blockage of cerebrospinal fluid flow)

Not every brain tumour requires immediate surgery. Some slow-growing tumours — particularly incidentally discovered low-grade lesions in patients without symptoms — may initially be managed with active surveillance (watchful waiting with serial MRI).

---

**Q: What is awake craniotomy, and why is it done?**

Awake craniotomy is a surgical technique in which the patient is kept awake and responsive during the critical phase of tumour removal. It is used when the tumour is located adjacent to or within eloquent brain areas — regions responsible for speech, language, movement, or sensation — where inadvertent damage would result in significant neurological deficit.

During the procedure, the patient is asked to perform specific tasks (such as naming objects, counting, or moving a limb) while the surgeon simultaneously maps the brain's functional boundaries and removes the tumour. If a surgical manoeuvre causes disruption to the task, the surgeon adjusts accordingly, preserving function.

Awake craniotomy is demanding for both the patient and the surgical team. It requires thorough preoperative preparation, strong patient-surgeon trust, and an experienced anaesthetic and nursing team. The outcome — a greater chance of preserving neurological function while maximising tumour removal — is well supported by evidence.

---

**Q: What are the risks of brain surgery?**

All surgery carries risk, and neurosurgery is no exception. The specific risks depend on the location and nature of the surgery, but general risks include bleeding, infection, neurological deficit (weakness, speech difficulty, sensory change), stroke, seizures, blood clot, and anaesthetic complications.

Dr Nor Faizal discusses risks transparently with every patient before surgery. He believes that informed consent is not a formality — it is a genuine process of shared decision-making. Understanding the risks, alongside the expected benefits and the risks of not operating, is essential for every patient facing a neurosurgical procedure.

---

**Q: How long is the recovery from brain surgery?**

Recovery varies considerably depending on the surgery performed, the patient's pre-operative neurological status, and individual factors. Many patients undergoing elective craniotomy are mobilised within one to two days of surgery and discharged within three to seven days, subject to clinical progress. Full functional recovery — including return to work — may take several weeks to months, particularly if the surgery involved eloquent brain regions.

Dr Nor Faizal will discuss expected recovery timelines in detail before any planned procedure, and will provide clear guidance on what to expect at each stage of recovery.

---

**Q: What is the difference between a biopsy and a craniotomy?**

A **biopsy** involves removing a small sample of tumour tissue — either through a small burr hole (stereotactic biopsy) or during a larger surgical exposure — for pathological examination. It is performed when a tissue diagnosis is needed but complete removal is not the immediate goal.

A **craniotomy** involves temporarily removing a section of the skull to access the brain and surgically resect (remove) the tumour. The extent of resection may range from partial (debulking) to complete (gross total resection), depending on the tumour's location and relationship to critical structures. A craniotomy may also include a biopsy if complete resection is not achievable.

## 3. About Pain Management

**Q: What is interventional pain management?**

Interventional pain management refers to a range of minimally invasive procedures designed to diagnose and treat pain conditions that have not responded adequately to medications, physiotherapy, or other conservative measures. These procedures target specific pain pathways — nerves, joints, or spinal structures — to reduce pain signals at their source.

Dr Nor Faizal's pain practice draws on his neurosurgical expertise to offer procedures that go beyond what a general pain physician can provide. His interventional repertoire includes epidural steroid injections, nerve root blocks, facet joint injections, radiofrequency ablation, spinal cord stimulation, and intrathecal drug delivery systems.

---

**Q: What conditions can be treated with interventional pain techniques?**

Interventional pain techniques are effective for a wide range of chronic pain conditions, including:

- Chronic back pain and neck pain
- Radiculopathy (nerve root pain — sciatica, cervical radiculopathy)
- Failed back surgery syndrome
- Neuropathic pain
- Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS)
- Cancer-related pain
- Trigeminal neuralgia
- Spinal stenosis-related pain
- Facet joint arthropathy

Not every patient is a candidate for every procedure. A thorough assessment is required before recommending any interventional treatment.

---

**Q: What is spinal cord stimulation?**

Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is a well-established interventional technique in which a small device is implanted near the spinal cord to deliver mild electrical impulses that modulate pain signals before they reach the brain. It is particularly effective for failed back surgery syndrome, neuropathic leg pain, and complex regional pain syndrome.

The procedure is typically done in two stages: a trial phase to confirm effectiveness, followed by permanent implantation if the trial is successful. Advances in SCS technology — including high-frequency stimulation and burst stimulation — have significantly improved outcomes in recent years.

---

**Q: Is interventional pain treatment a permanent solution?**

The degree and duration of pain relief varies between patients and procedures. Some interventions, such as radiofrequency ablation, provide relief for months to years and can be repeated. Spinal cord stimulation often provides sustained long-term pain reduction. Other procedures, such as epidural steroid injections, provide targeted relief that may need to be periodically repeated.

Interventional pain treatment is most effective when integrated within a broader pain management plan that may include medication optimisation, physiotherapy, psychological support, and lifestyle modification. The goal is to improve function and quality of life — not simply to suppress pain temporarily.

---

**Q: Is there a difference between a neurosurgeon and a pain specialist?**

Yes. A neurosurgeon is trained in the surgical management of conditions affecting the brain, spine, and peripheral nerves. A pain specialist may be an anaesthesiologist, physiatrist, neurologist, or other physician with additional training in pain medicine and interventional procedures.

Dr Nor Faizal is unusual in holding both qualifications: he is a trained neurosurgeon and a fellowship-trained interventional pain specialist. This dual expertise means he can evaluate patients with complex spinal and neurological pain conditions from both a surgical and an interventional perspective — and recommend the right treatment approach for each individual.

## 4. About Appointments and Process

**Q: Do I need a referral to see Dr Nor Faizal?**

No, a referral letter is not mandatory. Self-referred patients are welcome. However, if you have already been seen by a GP or another specialist, a referral letter containing your clinical history, investigations, and the referring doctor's concerns is very helpful as it allows Dr Nor Faizal's team to prepare for your consultation and review your records in advance.

---

**Q: How long will I wait for an appointment?**

Appointment availability varies. For routine consultations, an appointment can typically be arranged within a few days to two weeks. For urgent clinical situations — such as a newly identified brain lesion on imaging or rapidly progressing neurological symptoms — please indicate the urgency when contacting the clinic, and the team will make every effort to see you as soon as possible.

---

**Q: What happens if I need emergency care outside of clinic hours?**

If you experience a medical emergency outside of clinic hours, proceed immediately to the **Emergency Department at KPJ Tawakkal Specialist Hospital** or the nearest emergency facility, and call **999** if required. Do not wait for a clinic appointment in an emergency.

**Q: Can Dr Nor Faizal provide a second opinion on my diagnosis or treatment plan?**

Yes. Second opinion consultations are welcomed and treated with the same thoroughness as any new consultation. Please bring all available records, imaging CDs, pathology reports, and previous clinic letters. Dr Nor Faizal will review your case independently and provide his assessment. All information shared is treated with strict confidentiality.

## 5. After Treatment and Recovery

**Q: How often do I need follow-up after brain tumour surgery?**

Follow-up intervals after brain tumour surgery depend on the type and grade of tumour, the treatment received, and the clinical trajectory. Typically, post-operative MRI imaging is performed within 24 to 72 hours of surgery to assess the extent of resection, followed by further imaging at regular intervals (commonly three to six months) to monitor for tumour recurrence or progression.

Patients undergoing concurrent radiotherapy and chemotherapy will be followed up jointly by the neurosurgery, radiation oncology, and medical oncology teams. Dr Nor Faizal will outline the specific follow-up plan for your case at the time of discharge and review appointments.

---

**Q: What symptoms after surgery should prompt me to seek urgent review?**

After neurosurgery, please seek urgent review — attend the Emergency Department or call the clinic immediately — if you experience:

- New or significantly worsening headache
- Fever (temperature above 38°C)
- Redness, swelling, or discharge from the surgical wound
- New-onset seizure, or change in seizure frequency
- New weakness, numbness, or speech difficulty
- Confusion or altered consciousness
- Nausea and vomiting that cannot be controlled
- Vision changes

When in doubt, contact the clinic or attend the Emergency Department. It is always better to be assessed and reassured than to delay evaluation of a potentially serious complication.

---

**Q: Will I be able to drive after brain surgery?**

This depends on your clinical situation. In Malaysia, patients who have undergone brain surgery or who have been diagnosed with epilepsy or seizure disorder are required to comply with medical fitness-to-drive guidelines. As a general rule, you should not drive until you have been formally cleared to do so by your neurosurgeon, and until any mandatory seizure-free period (where applicable) has been fulfilled. Dr Nor Faizal will advise you specifically on driving during your post-operative consultations.

---

**Q: Can I return to work after brain surgery?**

Return to work depends on the nature of your job, the type of surgery performed, and your rate of recovery. Many patients with desk-based or sedentary work can return to work within four to eight weeks of elective craniotomy. Those in physically demanding or safety-sensitive roles may require a longer period of recovery and formal occupational assessment. Dr Nor Faizal will discuss return-to-work expectations as part of your recovery planning.

---

**Q: Where can I find support during treatment?**

Receiving a brain tumour diagnosis — or undergoing any serious neurosurgical treatment — is emotionally and psychologically demanding for patients and their families. Dr Nor Faizal encourages patients to access support through:

- **Hospital social work and counselling services** at KPJ Tawakkal
- **National patient support groups** for brain tumour and cancer patients in Malaysia
- **Palliative care services**, if symptom management and quality-of-life support are a priority
- **Peer support** — connecting with others who have lived experience of similar diagnoses

You do not have to face this alone, and asking for help is a sign of strength, not weakness.
