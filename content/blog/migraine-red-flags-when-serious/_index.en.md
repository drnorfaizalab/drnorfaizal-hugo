# Migraine vs Brain Tumour: Red Flags You Should Not Ignore

Let me begin with the reassurance that you deserve: the overwhelming majority of headaches are benign. Migraine affects approximately 1 in 7 people globally. Tension-type headache is even more prevalent. These are real, often debilitating conditions — but they are not harbingers of a tumour, an aneurysm, or a bleed. If you are reading this because you have headaches and are frightened, the statistical probability is firmly in your favour.

But probability is not certainty. And there is a small, critically important group of patients for whom a headache is the presenting symptom of something that requires urgent investigation. Knowing the difference — knowing the red flags — is information that could, without exaggeration, save a life. It may save yours, or that of someone you love.

## The Two Categories of Headache

Neurologists divide headaches into two fundamental categories:

**Primary headaches** are headaches where the headache itself is the disorder. There is no underlying structural or systemic cause. Migraine, tension-type headache, cluster headache, and a range of rarer primary syndromes all fall into this category. The vast majority of chronic headache sufferers fall here.

**Secondary headaches** are headaches caused by an underlying condition — a structural lesion (tumour, vascular malformation), infection, vascular event, raised intracranial pressure, or systemic illness. These headaches are symptoms, not the disease itself. The primary aim when encountering a secondary headache is to identify and treat the underlying cause.

The clinical challenge is that the pain of a secondary headache can, in its early stages, closely mimic a primary headache disorder. A meningioma can produce headaches that superficially resemble tension headache. A cerebellar tumour can generate symptoms that initially suggest vestibular migraine. This is why red flags exist: they are the clinical features that should prompt the clinician — and the informed patient — to look beyond the obvious.

## The SNOOP4 Framework

In headache medicine, the red flags are systematised through the SNOOP4 mnemonic — a framework I find invaluable both as a clinical tool and as a way of explaining risk to patients.

**S — Systemic Symptoms or Secondary Risk Factors**

Headache accompanied by fever, neck stiffness, rash, or weight loss demands investigation. These symptoms suggest infection (meningitis, encephalitis), systemic malignancy with brain metastases, or inflammatory conditions. A patient with a known cancer history who develops new headaches — even if they have had headaches before — must be assessed with that history firmly in mind. Similarly, a patient who is immunocompromised (due to HIV, organ transplant, or long-term steroid use) faces an expanded differential that includes opportunistic infections of the central nervous system.

**N — Neurological Symptoms or Signs**

Any headache accompanied by neurological abnormality — weakness of a limb, facial droop, speech difficulty, visual field defect, double vision, loss of coordination, or cognitive change — is a secondary headache until proven otherwise. These symptoms indicate that the headache is not an isolated event but part of a process affecting brain structure or function.

**O — Onset: Sudden or "Thunderclap"**

This is arguably the most critical red flag in headache medicine. A thunderclap headache is defined as a headache that reaches maximum intensity within one minute of onset — sometimes within seconds. Patients frequently describe it as "the worst headache of my life" or as feeling like being "hit on the back of the head." This presentation is subarachnoid haemorrhage (SAH) until proven otherwise.

Subarachnoid haemorrhage — bleeding into the space around the brain, most commonly from a ruptured cerebral aneurysm — carries a mortality rate of 30–40%, and a further proportion of survivors sustain significant disability. The thunderclap presentation is its calling card. Every patient presenting with a thunderclap headache requires emergency CT of the brain, and if the CT is negative, a lumbar puncture to look for blood products (xanthochromia) in the cerebrospinal fluid. A negative CT alone is not sufficient to exclude SAH in the hyperacute period.

I make this point with deliberate emphasis: if you or anyone with you develops a sudden, severe, maximum-intensity headache of a kind they have never experienced before, this is a medical emergency. Call for help immediately. Do not wait to see if it improves.

**O — Older Patient: New Headache After Age 50**

New-onset headache in a patient over 50 who has no prior headache history warrants investigation. The differential diagnosis in this age group is broader and more serious — including temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis), intracranial tumour (primary and metastatic), and cerebrovascular disease. Temporal arteritis specifically can cause sudden, irreversible visual loss if not treated promptly, and it is an eminently treatable condition when caught in time.

**P — Progressive Headache or Change in Pattern**

A headache that is clearly worsening over weeks or months — increasing in frequency, intensity, or duration — is not behaving like a primary headache disorder. Migraine fluctuates; it does not inexorably progress. A previously stable headache pattern that changes significantly (new associated symptoms, different character, different location) also warrants reassessment. This is one of the most important concepts I try to convey: the history of the headache over time is as important as any single episode.

**P — Postural Headache**

Headache that changes significantly with position — worsening when lying down or bending forward, improving when upright — may reflect raised intracranial pressure. Conversely, headache that is worst when standing and improves when lying flat suggests low cerebrospinal fluid pressure (intracranial hypotension), which can result from CSF leak. Both require investigation.

**P — Precipitated by Valsalva Manoeuvre**

Headache triggered or significantly worsened by coughing, sneezing, bearing down, or other manoeuvres that raise intracranial pressure (Valsalva manoeuvres) is a flag for posterior fossa pathology. A Chiari malformation — a structural abnormality in which brain tissue extends into the spinal canal — classically produces this pattern. Posterior fossa tumours can also present this way.

**P — Papilloedema**

Papilloedema is swelling of the optic disc, visible on examination of the retina, caused by raised intracranial pressure. Its presence confirms that the intracranial pressure is elevated and demands immediate investigation. Associated symptoms may include transient visual obscurations — brief episodes of visual dimming or greying, often precipitated by changes in posture.

## Morning Headaches and Brain Tumours

A specific pattern deserves its own mention: headache that is present on waking, particularly in the early morning hours, and improves through the day. This pattern reflects the normal physiological increase in intracranial pressure during sleep (associated with lying flat and the sleep-related increase in cerebral blood flow). A space-occupying lesion that is already pushing intracranial pressure toward its limit will make this morning peak symptomatic. Patients often describe these headaches as dull and frontal, accompanied by nausea. They are not universal features of brain tumours — many tumour patients have no morning headache at all — but when this pattern is present, it merits investigation.

## Headache with Visual Changes

Visual symptoms accompanying headache create a broad differential that warrants careful characterisation. The visual aura of migraine — typically a slowly expanding arc of flickering, shimmering light (fortification spectra) lasting 20–60 minutes and followed by headache — is a well-characterised and generally benign phenomenon. It follows a stereotyped pattern for each individual and does not vary in character from episode to episode.

What is not benign: a visual field defect that persists between headaches; double vision accompanying headache; sudden visual loss; or visual symptoms that are new and do not follow the typical aura pattern. These should prompt referral and imaging without delay. A pituitary macroadenoma, for example, can compress the optic chiasm, producing bitemporal visual field loss that the patient may initially notice only as difficulty reading or peripheral clumsiness — long before headache becomes prominent.

## What Imaging to Request

For any patient with red flag features, MRI brain with gadolinium contrast is the imaging of choice. It is more sensitive than CT for most parenchymal lesions and does not expose the patient to ionising radiation. CT brain without contrast remains the first-line investigation for suspected acute haemorrhage (including SAH in the acute phase) because of its speed and wide availability.

For suspected subarachnoid haemorrhage, CT angiography of the cerebral vasculature is used to identify an underlying aneurysm. For suspected temporal arteritis, ESR, CRP, and temporal artery biopsy form the diagnostic pathway. If you attend a specialist with a concerning headache, the appropriate imaging will be directed by the clinical picture. Self-requesting imaging without clinical context risks both over-investigation and under-investigation.

## A Message of Calibrated Reassurance

I want to return to where I began: most headaches are benign. I do not want this article to produce a generation of anxious migraine sufferers convinced that every attack portends disaster. Migraine is a genuinely disabling neurological condition that deserves better treatment than it typically receives — but it is not, in itself, dangerous.

The goal of knowing these red flags is not to generate anxiety but to generate appropriate action in the right circumstances. Know your headache pattern. Notice when it changes. Trust your instincts when something feels different. And if any of the features described here apply to you, please do not wait.
