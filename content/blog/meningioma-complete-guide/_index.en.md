# Meningioma Brain Tumour: The Complete Patient Guide

Receiving a diagnosis of meningioma is, for most patients, an experience that stops time. You may have come to the clinic with a persistent headache or a subtle change in vision, expecting reassurance, and left holding a sheaf of MRI images and a word you had never encountered before. I want this guide to be the first thing you read — before you fall into a spiral of internet searches, before well-meaning relatives begin forwarding alarming articles. My goal is to give you an honest, complete, and calm account of what a meningioma is, what it means for your life, and what modern neurosurgery can offer you.

## What Is a Meningioma?

The brain and spinal cord are enclosed by three protective layers of tissue called the meninges — the dura mater, arachnoid mater, and pia mater. A meningioma is a tumour that arises from cells within these membranes, most commonly from the arachnoid cap cells of the middle layer. Because it originates outside the brain substance itself, a meningioma is what we call an extra-axial tumour — it compresses the brain from without rather than arising within it.

This distinction matters enormously. It is why meningiomas are generally more amenable to complete surgical removal than tumours embedded within brain tissue, and it is one of the reasons that the prognosis for most meningiomas is considerably more favourable than patients initially fear.

Meningioma is the most common primary brain tumour, accounting for approximately 36–38% of all primary central nervous system tumours diagnosed each year. In my practice at KPJ Tawakkal, meningioma is consistently among the most frequent diagnoses I manage — and the majority of these patients, when treated appropriately, go on to live full and active lives.

## Who Gets Meningiomas?

Meningiomas are significantly more common in women than in men, with a ratio of approximately 2:1 for intracranial tumours and even higher for spinal meningiomas. The peak incidence is in the fifth to seventh decades of life, though meningiomas can and do occur at any age. The gender disparity has led researchers to investigate the role of progesterone and oestrogen receptors, which are expressed on many meningioma cells. This is also why some women notice that a meningioma grows more rapidly during pregnancy.

Established risk factors include prior radiation to the head (even low-dose radiation used decades ago for scalp conditions) and certain rare genetic syndromes, most notably Neurofibromatosis Type 2 (NF2), which predisposes individuals to multiple meningiomas. The vast majority of cases, however, occur sporadically with no identifiable hereditary cause.

## Tumour Grades: What Grade Means for You

The World Health Organisation (WHO) classifies meningiomas into three grades that reflect their biological behaviour:

**WHO Grade I (Benign):** This accounts for approximately 80% of all meningiomas. These tumours grow slowly, have a well-defined border, and are associated with an excellent prognosis after complete surgical removal. When I achieve what we call a Simpson Grade I or II resection — meaning complete removal of the tumour along with its dural attachment — the recurrence rate at ten years is under 10%.

**WHO Grade II (Atypical):** Representing roughly 18% of cases, atypical meningiomas show features of more active cell division and have a higher rate of recurrence. Complete surgical removal remains the primary treatment, but adjuvant radiotherapy is often recommended. These tumours require more vigilant long-term surveillance.

**WHO Grade III (Anaplastic/Malignant):** Accounting for approximately 2% of meningiomas, these are aggressive tumours with a behaviour more akin to a malignancy. Treatment combines aggressive surgery with radiotherapy, and the prognosis is considerably more guarded. Fortunately, this grade is genuinely rare.

## Recognising the Symptoms

Because meningiomas grow slowly — often over years or even decades — symptoms develop gradually and may be dismissed as the normal changes of ageing. The specific symptoms depend almost entirely on the tumour's location within the cranial vault.

**Convexity meningiomas** (on the outer surface of the brain) commonly present with focal seizures, weakness on one side of the body, or changes in personality if they lie over the frontal lobe.

**Parasagittal and falcine meningiomas** (along the midline structures) frequently cause weakness in one or both legs, and may present as a gait disturbance.

**Sphenoid wing meningiomas** can cause visual disturbance, proptosis (bulging of the eye), or facial pain and numbness.

**Olfactory groove meningiomas** are notorious for causing a gradual loss of sense of smell that patients often attribute to sinusitis, as well as personality and behavioural changes.

**Posterior fossa meningiomas** may present with problems of hearing, balance, facial sensation, or swallowing, depending on the precise location.

**Spinal meningiomas** — which occur predominantly in middle-aged women in the thoracic region — typically present with progressive limb weakness, sensory disturbance, and sometimes bladder or bowel dysfunction.

A small but important subset of meningiomas are discovered entirely incidentally — a patient has a scan for an unrelated reason and a small, asymptomatic tumour is found. This is an increasingly common scenario as cross-sectional imaging becomes more widely used.

## Diagnosis: What to Expect from Imaging

MRI with gadolinium contrast is the gold standard for diagnosing meningioma. The characteristic appearance — a sharply defined, intensely enhancing extra-axial mass with a "dural tail" of contrast extending along the meninges — is often distinctive enough that an experienced radiologist or neurosurgeon can make a confident provisional diagnosis from imaging alone.

In most cases, I do not require a biopsy before surgery if the clinical and radiological picture is clear. The pathological diagnosis is confirmed from the specimen removed at operation. Occasionally, for tumours in surgically challenging locations, I will recommend CT angiography to map the arterial supply to the tumour, which assists in planning both surgical approaches and, where appropriate, preoperative embolisation to reduce blood loss during surgery.

## Treatment Options

### Watchful Waiting

Not every meningioma requires immediate treatment. For small tumours (typically under 3 cm) that are incidentally discovered, show no evidence of oedema (swelling) in the surrounding brain, and produce no symptoms, a strategy of active surveillance with serial MRI scans is entirely appropriate. Many such tumours remain stable for years. In my practice at KPJ Tawakkal, I have patients I have been monitoring for over five years whose tumours have shown no meaningful growth. The decision to continue observation versus proceed with treatment is made collaboratively, accounting for tumour size, growth rate on serial imaging, the patient's age and overall health, and patient preference.

### Surgery

Surgical removal — craniotomy and tumour excision — remains the definitive treatment for symptomatic meningiomas and for tumours that demonstrate growth on surveillance imaging. The goals of surgery are to remove as much tumour as safely possible (ideally achieving complete resection), to obtain tissue for histological grading, and to relieve pressure on the brain and its associated structures.

The extent of resection is the single most important factor determining recurrence risk. In my practice, I use intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring routinely to protect eloquent cortical and subcortical structures. For tumours adjacent to critical areas — the motor cortex, language areas, optic apparatus, or major venous sinuses — careful preoperative planning and intraoperative navigation are essential.

Recovery from craniotomy for meningioma depends heavily on tumour location and the patient's preoperative condition. Most patients are mobilised within 24–48 hours of surgery. Hospital stay typically ranges from 4 to 7 days. I advise most of my working patients to plan for 6 to 8 weeks before returning to desk-based work, and longer for physically demanding roles.

### Stereotactic Radiosurgery

Stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS) — most commonly delivered as Gamma Knife or CyberKnife treatment — is an excellent option for meningiomas that are small (generally under 3 cm), surgically inaccessible, or where surgery carries unacceptable risk due to the patient's medical condition. SRS delivers a precisely focused, high dose of radiation to the tumour in a single session or a small number of sessions. It does not remove the tumour but rather arrests its growth. Control rates for Grade I meningiomas treated with SRS are excellent, exceeding 90% at ten years in well-selected cases.

## Prognosis and Recurrence

The prognosis for WHO Grade I meningioma, when completely resected, is excellent. The ten-year overall survival rate approaches that of the general age-matched population. Even incompletely resected Grade I tumours can be managed effectively with SRS to any residual.

The key message I give every patient: a diagnosis of meningioma, for most people, is not a death sentence. It is a condition that requires expert management, ongoing vigilance, and a partnership between you and your neurosurgeon. I have seen patients flourish for twenty years and beyond after surgery, returning fully to their professional and personal lives.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can meningiomas turn malignant?** A small proportion of Grade I tumours can dedifferentiate to Grade II or III over time, which is one reason ongoing surveillance after treatment is important. This, however, is the exception rather than the rule.

**Is there a hereditary risk?** Most meningiomas are sporadic. If you have multiple meningiomas or a strong family history, genetic counselling regarding NF2 and related syndromes is worthwhile.

**Can I fly after diagnosis?** In the absence of severe raised intracranial pressure, there is no absolute prohibition on air travel for most meningioma patients. Discuss this with your surgeon based on your specific circumstances.

**Should I stop hormonal therapy?** There is a theoretical concern regarding oestrogen and progesterone receptor-positive meningiomas and exogenous hormones. I discuss this individually with each patient; the evidence base for discontinuation is not definitive, but it is a conversation worth having.
