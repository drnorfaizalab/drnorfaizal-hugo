# Glioblastoma (GBM): Understanding Your Treatment Options

There are conversations in medicine that require a particular kind of courage — from the physician as much as from the patient. Glioblastoma is one of those conversations. I believe deeply that patients and their families deserve honesty delivered with care, and that understanding your diagnosis — truly understanding it, not just its name — is itself a form of power. This article will not soften the reality of GBM, but it will give you the most complete and current picture I can offer, and it will remind you that statistics are populations, not individuals.

## What Is Glioblastoma?

Glioblastoma multiforme — more accurately termed glioblastoma, or GBM — is classified as a WHO Grade IV astrocytoma, the most aggressive of the primary brain tumours. Unlike meningiomas, which arise from the protective membranes surrounding the brain, glioblastoma arises from within the brain tissue itself, specifically from astrocytes — the supportive glial cells of the central nervous system.

What makes glioblastoma so challenging is its fundamental biology. GBM is not a single tumour — it is a heterogeneous collection of genetically distinct cell populations within the same mass. Individual tumour cells infiltrate surrounding brain tissue along white matter tracts, making it impossible to achieve a truly clean surgical margin. This is the biological reason why, even with the most aggressive surgery, GBM cannot be cured by resection alone.

GBM accounts for approximately 14–15% of all primary brain tumours and roughly 45–50% of all malignant primary brain tumours. It occurs predominantly in adults, with a peak incidence in the sixth and seventh decades of life. Men are affected slightly more frequently than women. Most cases arise de novo — meaning they develop rapidly without a preceding lower-grade tumour — though a minority evolve from lower-grade gliomas over years.

## Recognising the Symptoms

GBM typically presents over weeks to a few months, reflecting its rapid growth rate. The most common symptoms include:

- **New-onset seizures** — often the first manifestation, particularly in tumours involving the temporal or frontal lobes
- **Progressive neurological deficit** — weakness in an arm or leg, speech difficulty, or changes in cognition, depending on the tumour's location
- **Headache** — often worse in the morning and associated with nausea, reflecting raised intracranial pressure
- **Personality and behaviour changes** — particularly with frontal lobe involvement, these are often noticed first by family members
- **Cognitive decline** — difficulties with memory, concentration, or executive function

The velocity of symptom development is often diagnostically telling. A headache that has been present for six years is rarely due to GBM. Symptoms that have progressed meaningfully over six weeks are a different matter entirely.

## Diagnosis: The Role of MRI and Tissue

MRI with gadolinium contrast is the primary diagnostic imaging modality. GBM has a characteristic appearance: an irregularly enhancing mass with a necrotic (non-enhancing) centre, surrounded by extensive oedema. This "ring enhancement" pattern, while highly suggestive of GBM, can occasionally be mimicked by other conditions including brain metastases, abscess, or primary CNS lymphoma. Tissue diagnosis is therefore almost always essential.

In the majority of cases, surgery serves both a therapeutic purpose (reducing tumour bulk) and a diagnostic one (providing tissue for histopathology and molecular analysis). In patients where surgery carries prohibitive risk, stereotactic biopsy — a minimally invasive, image-guided needle biopsy — can be performed to obtain tissue.

## The Molecular Landscape: Why Markers Matter

Two molecular markers have become central to the modern classification and management of GBM:

**IDH Mutation Status:** The 2021 WHO Classification of CNS Tumours now defines "IDH-wildtype glioblastoma" as the canonical, most aggressive form. IDH-mutant high-grade gliomas, while still serious, carry a meaningfully better prognosis and are now classified differently. This distinction is not merely academic — it changes the conversation about prognosis significantly.

**MGMT Promoter Methylation:** The MGMT gene encodes a DNA repair enzyme. When the promoter of this gene is methylated (chemically silenced), tumour cells are less able to repair the DNA damage caused by temozolomide chemotherapy — which means they are more sensitive to it. Patients whose tumours carry MGMT promoter methylation have a substantially better response to the Stupp Protocol and a meaningfully better prognosis. Approximately 40–45% of GBM tumours are MGMT methylated. When I tell a patient their MGMT status, it is one of the most clinically significant pieces of information I can share.

## The Stupp Protocol: Current Standard of Care

The current standard treatment for glioblastoma was established by Roger Stupp and colleagues in their landmark 2005 trial and remains the foundation of care today. The Stupp Protocol comprises three sequential elements:

**1. Maximal Safe Surgical Resection**

Surgery is the first and foundational step. The goal is to remove as much tumour as safely possible — what we call maximal safe resection — without causing new neurological deficits that would diminish quality of life. Every study examining this question has shown that greater extent of resection correlates with longer survival. The relationship is not linear, but achieving a resection of 90% or more of the contrast-enhancing tumour volume carries meaningful benefit.

To maximise the extent of resection while protecting function, I employ several techniques: intraoperative MRI to assess real-time resection extent, fluorescence-guided surgery using 5-aminolevulinic acid (5-ALA), which causes GBM cells to glow pink under specific light, and continuous neurophysiological monitoring. For tumours in or near eloquent brain areas — language, motor function, sensory processing — I perform awake craniotomy.

**Awake Craniotomy** deserves a specific explanation because many patients are understandably alarmed when it is proposed. Under carefully managed sedation combined with local anaesthesia, the patient is kept conscious during the critical phase of tumour removal. This allows real-time assessment of language and motor function as I operate, dramatically reducing the risk of causing a new deficit while permitting more aggressive tumour removal. In my practice at KPJ Tawakkal, I have found that patients who are well-prepared for awake craniotomy — who understand exactly what to expect — tolerate the procedure with remarkable composure.

**2. Concurrent Radiotherapy and Temozolomide**

Following recovery from surgery (typically within 4–6 weeks), patients commence a 6-week course of daily radiotherapy delivered to the tumour bed, given concurrently with oral temozolomide chemotherapy taken daily throughout the radiation course. This combined approach was the central innovation of the Stupp Protocol and demonstrated a significant survival advantage over radiotherapy alone.

**3. Adjuvant Temozolomide**

Following the concurrent phase, patients receive 6 cycles of temozolomide given on 5 days out of every 28, for a total of approximately 6 months. The drug is generally well tolerated, with the principal concerns being myelosuppression (reduction in blood cell counts) and fatigue.

## Additional Treatment Options

**Bevacizumab (Avastin):** This anti-angiogenic monoclonal antibody, which targets the blood vessel growth that tumours depend upon, is approved in some countries for recurrent GBM. It reliably improves progression-free survival and can significantly reduce tumour-related oedema, sometimes allowing steroid doses to be reduced. Its effect on overall survival has been more modest in clinical trials, but its quality-of-life benefits are real and meaningful.

**Tumour Treating Fields (TTFields/Optune):** This is a wearable device that delivers alternating electric fields to the scalp through transducer arrays, disrupting tumour cell division. The EF-14 trial demonstrated a statistically significant improvement in overall survival when TTFields were added to standard adjuvant temozolomide. The device requires continuous wear (ideally 18+ hours per day) and represents a significant lifestyle commitment, but for motivated patients with good performance status, it is a legitimate addition to the treatment plan.

**Clinical Trials:** GBM research is intensely active. Areas of investigation include immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cell therapy, personalised vaccine approaches), targeted molecular therapies based on specific tumour mutations, and enhanced drug delivery methods. I discuss clinical trial eligibility with all eligible patients. Participation in a trial does not mean receiving inferior care — it means contributing to the advancement that will one day change this diagnosis for those who follow.

## Prognosis: An Honest Conversation

I will not minimise this. The median overall survival for IDH-wildtype GBM treated with the Stupp Protocol is approximately 14–16 months. Median means that half of patients do better, and half do worse. The two-year survival rate is approximately 26–33%; the five-year survival rate is roughly 5–10%.

But median survival is a population statistic. I have patients in my practice who are 3 and 4 years out from their GBM diagnosis, working, travelling, attending their children's milestones. MGMT methylation, IDH status, age, performance status, and extent of resection all influence individual outcomes in ways that population statistics cannot fully capture. When I sit with a newly diagnosed patient, I share what the data shows, but I am careful to distinguish between a prognosis for a population and a prediction for a person.

## Quality of Life and Palliative Care

Quality of life is not a concession — it is a treatment goal. From the first consultation, I consider not only how to extend life but how to protect the life being lived. This means carefully balancing treatment intensity against functional impact, managing symptoms (seizures, headache, fatigue, steroid-related complications) proactively, and involving palliative care specialists early — not as a signal of giving up, but as a specialist service that exists to reduce suffering and support both patient and family throughout the entire illness trajectory.

For many patients and families, the hardest conversations are not with the neurosurgeon but with each other. My advice: begin those conversations early. Talk about what matters most. Talk about what you would and would not want if the situation changes. Write these things down. Share them with your medical team.

## A Message to Families

Those who love a person with GBM carry their own extraordinary burden. They need support, honest information, and space to grieve even while their person is still present. If there is one thing I would ask every family: please bring your questions to the consultation. There are no questions too basic or too difficult. This is a partnership — between patient, family, and the clinical team — and it works best when communication is open in all directions.
