# Pain as a Diagnostic Gateway: When Your Headache Is More Than Stress

Pain is the body's most eloquent signal. It is also, I have come to believe, one of the most underestimated — both by those who experience it and, occasionally, by those who are meant to investigate it. In my combined practice as a neurosurgeon and interventional pain specialist, I occupy a position that gives me an unusual perspective: I see what happens when pain is managed without a diagnosis, and I see what is discovered when pain is taken seriously as a diagnostic clue.

This article is for the patient who has been told their headache is stress, their back pain is posture, and their leg pain is ageing. Some of that advice will be correct. Some of it will not. Knowing which category you fall into may be the most important thing you do for your health.

## The Diagnostic Moat

I use the term "diagnostic moat" to describe a pattern I encounter repeatedly in clinical practice. A patient has been living with pain — perhaps for months, sometimes for years. They have taken analgesics. They have tried physiotherapy. They have been reassured. And during all that time, a structural or pathological cause for their pain has gone unidentified, sometimes progressing silently in the background.

Pain management is not merely a service for symptom relief. When practiced with diagnostic rigour, it is a gateway — often the primary gateway — to uncovering serious underlying neurological conditions. A meningioma may be found because someone finally ordered an MRI for persistent headaches that had been attributed to tension. A spinal cord tumour may be discovered because a pain physician noted that the distribution of a patient's leg pain was anatomically inconsistent with a simple disc problem. A cerebral venous sinus thrombosis may be identified because a headache specialist recognised that a patient's positional headaches warranted vascular imaging.

Treating pain without seeking its source is not just incomplete medicine — in some circumstances, it is dangerous medicine.

## What Different Types of Pain Are Telling You

Pain is not a uniform experience. Its character, distribution, timing, and modifying factors are all data points that carry diagnostic weight.

**Nociceptive pain** — the familiar pain of tissue injury, inflammation, or mechanical stress — typically has a clear relationship to movement or position, responds reasonably to standard analgesics, and is proportionate to identifiable physical findings. This is the pain of a muscle strain, an arthritic joint, or a healing wound. While it may be severe and debilitating, its cause is usually accessible to investigation.

**Neuropathic pain** behaves differently. It burns, shocks, or radiates in patterns that follow nerve distributions. Patients describe it as electric, stabbing, or "like hot water under the skin." It is frequently accompanied by sensory changes — numbness, tingling, heightened sensitivity to light touch. Neuropathic pain is the pain of a damaged, compressed, or irritated nerve — and that irritation has a source. The source may be a prolapsed disc compressing a nerve root, a tumour infiltrating the brachial or lumbosacral plexus, diabetic neuropathy, or post-herpetic neuralgia. Each of these requires a different diagnostic approach and a different treatment strategy.

**Referred pain** follows patterns that are counterintuitive but consistent. The heart refers pain to the left arm and jaw. The diaphragm refers pain to the shoulder tip. A cervical spine problem can refer pain into the arm or even the chest. Understanding referred pain patterns is fundamental to not being misled by where the pain is felt as opposed to where it originates.

## When a Headache Might Be a Tumour

The vast majority of headaches are primary — meaning they are the disorder itself, not a symptom of something else. Migraine, tension-type headache, and cluster headache collectively account for the overwhelming majority of headaches seen in clinical practice. I am not writing this to alarm the many patients who experience benign headaches. I am writing it for the small but significant minority for whom headache is the body's way of announcing something more serious.

Tumour-related headaches tend to have specific characteristics. They are often progressive — worsening week on week rather than fluctuating as migraine does. They are frequently worse in the morning (related to the physiological rise in intracranial pressure that occurs when lying flat during sleep). They may be associated with nausea and vomiting disproportionate to their severity. They may be accompanied by neurological symptoms — visual changes, weakness, cognitive alteration, or personality change. A headache that wakes the patient from sleep warrants attention.

The headache of a meningioma may be present for years before the tumour causes any other symptom, simply because of the slow growth rate. The headache of a high-grade glioma may progress from first onset to severe disability in a matter of weeks. Both are worth investigating when the clinical picture is not fitting the profile of a primary headache disorder.

## Back Pain and Spinal Cord Compression

Back pain is extraordinarily common — it is the leading cause of disability worldwide, and the vast majority of cases are mechanical in origin. But among the millions of people with back pain, a meaningful subset harbour serious pathology: spinal cord compression from a tumour (primary or metastatic), cauda equina syndrome from a large disc prolapse, or vertebral instability from infection or malignant infiltration.

The features that distinguish benign mechanical back pain from something that requires urgent investigation include: pain that is severe and unrelenting, particularly at night; pain that is progressive rather than intermittent; pain accompanied by neurological symptoms (leg weakness, sensory changes, bladder or bowel disturbance); pain in the context of a known cancer diagnosis; and back pain accompanied by constitutional symptoms such as weight loss, fever, or night sweats.

Cauda equina syndrome — compression of the nerve roots at the base of the spinal canal — is a surgical emergency. The symptoms include saddle-area numbness (numbness in the area that would contact a saddle), bilateral leg weakness, and loss of bladder or bowel control. If you or someone you know develops these symptoms, seek emergency care immediately. Hours matter.

## The Five Questions to Ask About Your Pain

When I assess a patient with pain in my clinic, I structure my thinking around five core questions. I encourage patients to apply these questions to their own experience before seeking medical advice, because the answers will make the consultation more productive:

**1. Where exactly is the pain, and does it move?** Can you point to it with one finger, or is it diffuse? Does it radiate — and if so, in what direction? Radiation that follows a nerve distribution (down the back of the leg to the foot, or down the arm to specific fingers) is diagnostically significant.

**2. What does the pain feel like?** Is it aching, burning, stabbing, electric? Does it throb? These descriptors help distinguish nociceptive from neuropathic pain and narrow the differential diagnosis considerably.

**3. What makes it better or worse?** Mechanical pain typically worsens with movement and improves with rest. Pain that is equally present at rest and with activity, or that is worse at night, is a different matter. Pain that is worsened by lying flat or by bearing down (Valsalva manoeuvre — coughing, sneezing, straining) may reflect raised intracranial pressure.

**4. How has it changed over time?** A stable pain with no functional deterioration over two years is biologically different from a pain that has been progressively worsening over six months. Progression is, in medicine, a warning sign.

**5. Are there any associated symptoms?** This is perhaps the most important question. Pain accompanied by numbness, weakness, visual disturbance, confusion, bladder or bowel change, unexplained weight loss, or fever is pain that has declared its intent to be taken seriously.

## When to Escalate from GP to Specialist

Your general practitioner is the right first point of contact for most pain presentations, and a good GP will conduct the appropriate initial assessment, investigations, and treatment trial. The decision to refer to a specialist — whether neurologist, neurosurgeon, or pain physician — should be prompted by: failure to respond to appropriate first-line treatment; the presence of any of the red flag features described above; neurological symptoms or signs accompanying the pain; progressive worsening despite treatment; or clinical uncertainty about the underlying cause.

## Why Treating Pain Without Diagnosis Is Dangerous

I want to be direct about this: prescribing opioids, steroid injections, or even physiotherapy in the presence of an undiagnosed structural lesion can mask the very symptoms that would prompt timely investigation. It can delay the identification of a tumour, a vascular malformation, or a spinal cord compression at a stage when intervention would have been most effective. It can create a false sense of reassurance — for patient and physician alike — that the situation is being managed, when in reality it is being deferred.

Pain management done well is diagnostic medicine with a therapeutic dimension. It asks: why is this person in pain? It does not accept "stress" or "normal ageing" as a sufficient answer until all other possibilities have been reasonably excluded.
