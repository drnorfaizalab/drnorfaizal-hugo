# Pain as a Diagnostic Gateway: When Your Headache Is More Than Stress

Pain is the body's most eloquent signal — and one of the most underestimated. As both a neurosurgeon and interventional pain specialist, I see what happens when pain is managed without a diagnosis, and what is discovered when it is taken seriously as a diagnostic clue.

This article is for the patient told their headache is stress, their back pain is posture, and their leg pain is ageing. Some of that advice is correct. Some is not.

## The Diagnostic Moat

I use the term "diagnostic moat" to describe a pattern I see repeatedly. A patient has lived with pain for months or years. They've taken analgesics, tried physiotherapy, been reassured. And throughout that time, a structural cause has gone unidentified — sometimes progressing silently in the background.

Pain management done with diagnostic rigour is often the primary gateway to uncovering serious neurological conditions. A meningioma found because someone finally ordered an MRI for persistent headaches. A spinal cord tumour discovered because the pain distribution was anatomically inconsistent with a simple disc problem. A cerebral venous sinus thrombosis identified because positional headaches warranted vascular imaging.

Treating pain without seeking its source is not just incomplete medicine — in some circumstances, it is dangerous medicine.

## What Different Pains Are Telling You

**Nociceptive pain** — tissue injury, inflammation, mechanical stress — has a clear relationship to movement, responds to standard analgesics, and is proportionate to identifiable findings. Its cause is usually accessible to investigation.

**Neuropathic pain** burns, shocks, or radiates along nerve distributions. Electric, stabbing, "like hot water under the skin." Often accompanied by numbness, tingling, or heightened sensitivity. This is the pain of a damaged or compressed nerve — with a source. A prolapsed disc, a tumour infiltrating the brachial plexus, diabetic neuropathy, post-herpetic neuralgia. Each requires a different diagnostic approach.

**Referred pain** follows counterintuitive but consistent patterns. The heart refers to the left arm and jaw. The diaphragm to the shoulder tip. A cervical spine problem to the arm or chest. Understanding referred pain is fundamental to not being misled by where pain is felt versus where it originates.

## When a Headache Might Be a Tumour

The vast majority of headaches are primary — the disorder itself, not a symptom. I am not writing this to alarm those with benign headaches. I am writing for the small minority for whom headache is the body announcing something more serious.

Tumour-related headaches are often progressive — worsening week on week. Frequently worse in the morning (from the physiological rise in intracranial pressure when lying flat). May be accompanied by disproportionate nausea, vomiting, visual changes, weakness, or personality change. A headache that wakes the patient from sleep warrants attention.

## Back Pain and Spinal Cord Compression

Most back pain is mechanical and resolves conservatively. But among the millions affected, a meaningful subset harbour serious pathology: spinal cord compression from tumour, cauda equina syndrome from a large disc, or vertebral instability from infection.

Red flags: pain severe and unrelenting especially at night; progressive rather than intermittent; accompanied by leg weakness, sensory changes, or bladder/bowel disturbance; in a patient with known cancer; or with weight loss, fever, or night sweats.

**Cauda equina syndrome** — saddle numbness, bilateral leg weakness, and loss of bladder or bowel control — is a surgical emergency. Hours matter. Seek emergency care immediately.

## Five Questions to Ask About Your Pain

1. **Where is it, and does it move?** Radiation following a nerve distribution is diagnostically significant.
2. **What does it feel like?** Aching, burning, stabbing, electric? Helps distinguish nociceptive from neuropathic pain.
3. **What makes it better or worse?** Mechanical pain worsens with movement. Pain equally present at rest, or worse at night, is a different matter.
4. **How has it changed over time?** Progression over months is a warning sign.
5. **Are there associated symptoms?** Numbness, weakness, visual disturbance, confusion, bladder/bowel change, weight loss, or fever — these demand investigation.

## When to See a Specialist

Your GP is the right first contact. Refer to a specialist when: first-line treatment fails; red flags are present; neurological symptoms accompany the pain; pain is progressively worsening; or diagnostic uncertainty remains.

In my practice at KPJ Tawakkal, the first step is always diagnostic — understanding why the pain exists before deciding how to treat it.

## Why Treating Pain Without Diagnosis Is Dangerous

Opioids, steroid injections, or physiotherapy in the presence of an undiagnosed structural lesion can mask the very symptoms that would prompt timely investigation. They delay identification of tumour, vascular malformation, or spinal cord compression at the stage when intervention is most effective. They create false reassurance — for patient and physician alike — that the situation is being managed, when in reality it is being deferred.

Pain management done well asks: *why is this person in pain?* It does not accept "stress" or "normal ageing" as sufficient answers until all other possibilities have been reasonably excluded.
