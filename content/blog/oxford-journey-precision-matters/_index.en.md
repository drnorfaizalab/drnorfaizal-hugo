# My Oxford Journey: Why Precision Neurosurgery Changes Everything

There is a moment that every surgeon remembers: the first time you understand that what you are doing is not merely technical work. That the instrument in your hand, held at a particular angle, exerting a particular pressure, is the difference between a life reclaimed and a life diminished. For me, that moment crystallised not in Malaysia, but in Oxford — in an operating theatre at the John Radcliffe Hospital, watching a consultant neurosurgeon navigate a eloquent cortex tumour with a patience and economy of movement that seemed to slow time itself.

I returned to Malaysia changed. That is the only word that fits. This is the story of what changed, and why it matters to every patient I see today.

## The Decision to Go

The decision to pursue a fellowship abroad is never simple. By the time I was considering Oxford, I had already completed my neurosurgical training in Malaysia — years of demanding, high-volume work that gave me a solid technical foundation. I could operate. I was competent. Competence, in neurosurgery, is not the same as excellence, and I knew it.

The world's leading neurosurgical centres — Oxford, London, Toronto, Pittsburgh, Zurich — had accumulated decades of experience in subspecialty areas that were still developing in Malaysia. Neuro-oncology, awake craniotomy, advanced neuromonitoring, complex skull base surgery — these were fields where the gap between what I had learned and what was being practiced at the frontier was real and significant. I wanted to stand at that frontier. Not for the credential, though the credential matters in how patients perceive their care. For what I would learn, and what I could bring home.

The John Radcliffe Hospital in Oxford is one of the most distinguished neurosurgical centres in the United Kingdom. It sits within a university environment of extraordinary intellectual intensity, where clinical work is continuously interrogated by research, where the question "why do we do it this way?" is not impertinence but expectation. Walking through those doors for the first time, I was acutely aware of arriving somewhere that had shaped modern neurosurgery.

## What Oxford Taught Me

The first thing Oxford taught me was about restraint.

There is a principle — spoken and unspoken — that pervades the culture of the best surgical centres: operate only when necessary, but when you operate, operate with complete commitment to excellence. The temptation in surgery is always toward action. Surgery feels decisive. It feels like doing something. But the Oxford surgeons I trained under were deeply, almost philosophically, suspicious of unnecessary intervention. They knew that every craniotomy, however skilled, carries risk — of haemorrhage, of oedema, of infection, of neurological deficit, of the unpredictable. Their restraint was not timidity. It was the discipline of people who had seen enough outcomes to understand that the indications for surgery must be airtight before the first incision is made.

I brought this principle home. In my practice at KPJ Tawakkal, I have patients who come expecting surgery and leave with a surveillance plan. I have patients who come expecting surveillance and, after a thorough review, leave with a surgical date. The key is that neither decision is default — both are deliberate.

## Learning Awake Craniotomy

The procedure that most profoundly shaped my practice during the fellowship was awake craniotomy. The concept is straightforward; the execution is anything but. Under carefully titrated anaesthesia and local scalp anaesthesia, the patient is kept conscious during the period of tumour resection in or adjacent to eloquent brain — the areas governing language, movement, memory. By maintaining consciousness, the surgeon can continuously verify, in real time, that the resection is not encroaching on function. The patient speaks, moves their limbs, names objects, reads sentences — and if they falter, the surgeon adjusts.

The procedure demands a team: a neurosurgeon skilled in cortical mapping and real-time intraoperative decision-making, an anaesthesiologist expert in the specific sedation protocols, a neuropsychologist to conduct the intraoperative assessments, a scrub team that understands the unusual dynamic of an awake theatre. It demands, most of all, that the patient is prepared — not merely consented in the technical legal sense, but genuinely prepared for what they will experience.

I spent considerable time in Oxford learning not only the surgical technique of awake craniotomy but the preparation protocol — how to explain the procedure, how to rehearse the intraoperative tasks with the patient in advance, how to build the trust that allows someone to lie on an operating table, consciously aware, while a surgeon works on their brain. Done well, patients routinely describe the experience as less frightening than they anticipated. I have had patients tell me it was the strangest and most extraordinary experience of their lives, and that they are grateful it was possible.

At KPJ Tawakkal, awake craniotomy is now a procedure I perform regularly for tumours in eloquent locations. For many patients, it is the difference between a surgery that removes the tumour and preserves their function, and a surgery that removes the tumour at the cost of their ability to speak, move, or be the person they were before.

## Neuromonitoring and the Invisible Safety Net

Alongside awake craniotomy, I immersed myself in comprehensive intraoperative neurophysiological monitoring — the continuous, real-time electrophysiological surveillance of the brain and spinal cord during surgery. Evoked potentials, motor-evoked potentials, electromyography, electroencephalography — these technologies create an invisible safety net, alerting the surgical team to changes in neural function before clinical deficit has occurred, in time to intervene.

In Oxford, these monitoring modalities were not optional extras — they were standard care for the appropriate procedures. The expectation that a complex craniotomy or spinal cord surgery would proceed without comprehensive monitoring would have been received with genuine surprise. Bringing this standard home to Malaysia was not merely a matter of installing equipment. It was a matter of building a team — training the neurophysiology technologists, educating the anaesthetic staff on how to adjust their technique to permit interpretable recordings, and integrating the monitoring data into real-time intraoperative decision-making.

This is the unglamorous but essential work of raising standards: it is not one procedure or one technology, but a system. Oxford showed me what the system looks like when it is fully realised.

## The Gap in Malaysia and Why It Matters

I returned to Malaysia with a clear-eyed view of where we stood as a national neurosurgical community. Malaysia has excellent neurosurgeons. We have capable hospitals, trained staff, and a neurosurgical workforce that manages high volumes of complex pathology. But subspecialty depth — the kind of depth that comes from a centre that does 50 awake craniotomies a year, that runs a dedicated neuro-oncology multidisciplinary team, that has a clinical trial infrastructure — was less developed than it needed to be.

The consequences of this gap are real and personal. A Malaysian patient with a glioma in the speech area who cannot access awake craniotomy may receive a less complete resection than their tumour biology would otherwise allow. A patient with a complex skull base tumour who is told there are no local options and referred abroad carries not only the financial burden of international travel but the psychological and logistical burden of navigating a foreign healthcare system in the most frightening period of their life.

I believe that every Malaysian patient deserves access to world-class neurosurgical care without leaving Malaysia. This is not naive idealism — I have seen what is possible when the training, the equipment, and the institutional commitment are aligned. I have been part of building that alignment, and I remain committed to extending it.

## My Clinical Philosophy

If I had to distil what Oxford confirmed and deepened in me into its simplest form, it would be three words: Discipline. Simplicity. Elegance.

**Discipline** means adherence to evidence-based practice, rigorous preoperative planning, and the willingness to say "not yet" or "not this way" when the data does not support intervention. It means continuous learning — reading the literature, attending meetings, questioning my own outcomes. It means that a busy clinic schedule does not become an excuse for shortcuts.

**Simplicity** means resisting the surgical ego that performs complexity for its own sake. The best surgery is often the most efficient surgery — the one that achieves the goal with the fewest steps, the least brain retraction, the shortest operative time. Unnecessary complexity in the operating room translates directly into increased risk for the patient. Simple, purposeful, effective.

**Elegance** is the quality I find hardest to teach but most recognisable when I see it. It is the economy of movement of a surgeon who knows exactly where they are going. It is the absence of hesitation. It is the calm that comes from thorough preparation meeting genuine skill. I saw it daily in Oxford. I strive for it every time I operate.

## A Message to Malaysian Patients

If you are reading this as a Malaysian patient — whether you have a diagnosis, are awaiting investigation, or are simply trying to understand your options — I want you to know something directly.

You do not have to go to London or Singapore or the United States to receive care that meets global standards. You deserve that care here, in Kuala Lumpur, close to your family, in your language, within a healthcare system that you understand. The knowledge and the technique that exist at the world's leading neurosurgical centres can be — and have been — brought home.

My practice at KPJ Tawakkal Specialist Hospital is built on this conviction. Every protocol I follow, every monitoring standard I maintain, every surgical decision I make is informed by training at one of the world's great neurosurgical institutions and by a commitment to bringing those standards to every patient who walks through the door, regardless of where they have come from.

Oxford taught me what excellence looks like. Malaysia is where I practice it.
