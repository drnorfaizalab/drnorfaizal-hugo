## Not Every Headache Is Dangerous — But Some Symptoms Cannot Wait

The vast majority of headaches, back pain episodes, and moments of dizziness are benign — caused by tension, dehydration, poor posture, stress, or fatigue. They are uncomfortable, sometimes debilitating, but not dangerous. They do not require a neurosurgeon.

But some neurological symptoms are different. They are the brain and nervous system's way of communicating that something is wrong — and in those cases, delay can have serious, sometimes irreversible consequences.

This checklist is designed to help you understand the difference: to know when you need emergency care right now, when you should see a specialist within days to weeks, and when you can manage your symptoms conservatively with reassurance.

**This checklist is a guide, not a diagnostic tool.** It does not replace a clinical assessment. If you are in any doubt about your symptoms, please seek medical attention.

---

## RED FLAG Symptoms — Seek Emergency Care Immediately

These symptoms require immediate evaluation at an Emergency Department or by calling **999**. Do not drive yourself. Do not wait for a clinic appointment. Do not manage these symptoms at home.

---

### Thunderclap Headache — Sudden, Severe, "Worst of My Life"

A headache that reaches maximum severity within seconds to minutes, often described as a sudden "explosion" or "crack" inside the head, is a medical emergency until proven otherwise. This presentation is the classic warning sign of a **subarachnoid haemorrhage** — bleeding from a ruptured aneurysm on the surface of the brain.

**What to do:** Call 999 or go directly to the Emergency Department. Do not take pain relief and go to sleep. Do not wait to see if it improves.

---

### Progressive Neurological Deficit

Any weakness, numbness, or loss of function that is worsening over hours or days — particularly weakness of an arm, leg, or one side of the body — may indicate a stroke, a rapidly growing brain lesion, or spinal cord compression.

**Signs to watch for:**
- Arm or leg weakness that is getting worse
- Facial drooping on one side
- Difficulty gripping or lifting objects that is new or worsening
- Numbness spreading from one area to a larger region
- Loss of bladder or bowel control with back pain (urgent spinal emergency)

**What to do:** Emergency Department immediately. In the case of sudden stroke symptoms — face drooping, arm weakness, speech difficulty — the **FAST acronym** applies: Face, Arms, Speech, Time. Call 999 immediately.

---

### New-Onset Seizure

A first seizure in an adult — with no prior history of epilepsy — is a neurological emergency. It may indicate a brain tumour, an abscess, a bleed, a metabolic disturbance, or other serious pathology. Even if the person appears fully recovered after the seizure, urgent neuroimaging and specialist evaluation are required.

**What to do:** Call 999 if the seizure is ongoing or if the person does not fully recover within a few minutes. For a completed first seizure with full recovery, proceed to the Emergency Department promptly for assessment. Do not drive until formally cleared by a doctor.

---

### Loss of Consciousness

Unexplained loss of consciousness — particularly if prolonged, recurrent, or associated with other neurological symptoms — requires urgent evaluation to exclude brain pathology, cardiac arrhythmia, and other serious causes.

**What to do:** Emergency Department assessment. If the person is not responding or cannot be roused, call 999 immediately.

---

### Sudden Loss of Vision

Sudden painless loss of vision in one or both eyes, or a sudden visual field defect (loss of part of the visual field), may indicate a vascular event affecting the optic nerve, retina, or visual cortex.

**What to do:** Emergency Department immediately. Time-sensitive conditions such as central retinal artery occlusion and posterior circulation stroke require urgent intervention.

---

### Sudden Onset Confusion or Personality Change

Rapid-onset confusion, disorientation, agitation, or marked personality change — particularly in a person who was functioning normally — may indicate encephalitis, a metabolic disturbance, a rapidly expanding brain lesion, or a neurological emergency.

**What to do:** Emergency Department assessment immediately, particularly if the change is acute.

---

### Weakness or Numbness on One Side of the Body

Unilateral weakness or sensory loss — affecting the arm, leg, or face on one side — is a hallmark of a brain lesion or stroke affecting the contralateral hemisphere. If sudden in onset, this is a stroke until proven otherwise.

**What to do:** Call 999 if symptoms are sudden and severe. Proceed to Emergency Department urgently for any new unilateral weakness or numbness, even if mild.

---

### Cauda Equina Syndrome (Spinal Emergency)

A combination of lower back pain, weakness or numbness in both legs, and loss of bladder or bowel control (inability to urinate or uncontrolled incontinence) constitutes a spinal emergency known as cauda equina syndrome, caused by compression of the nerve roots at the base of the spinal cord.

**What to do:** This is a surgical emergency. Go to the Emergency Department immediately. Delay in surgical decompression can result in permanent paralysis and permanent loss of bladder and bowel function.

## YELLOW FLAG Symptoms — See a Specialist Within 1–2 Weeks

These symptoms do not require emergency management but should not be indefinitely ignored. They warrant evaluation by a specialist — a neurologist or neurosurgeon — within one to two weeks to exclude a significant underlying cause.

---

### Persistent Headaches With a New Pattern

Headaches that are new, or that represent a significant change from your usual headache pattern, particularly if they:

- Are worse in the morning or when lying down
- Are progressively worsening over weeks
- Wake you from sleep
- Are accompanied by nausea and vomiting not explained by another cause
- Are associated with visual disturbance

A change in headache character is more concerning than long-standing headaches of the same quality. A brain tumour classically causes headaches that worsen with positional changes or over time — though headaches are a non-specific symptom and are most often benign.

**What to do:** Book an appointment with Dr Nor Faizal or your GP for assessment and appropriate neuroimaging.

---

### Back Pain With Leg Weakness or Numbness (Radiculopathy)

Back pain or neck pain accompanied by weakness, numbness, or tingling radiating down an arm or leg suggests nerve root compression — most commonly from a disc herniation or spinal stenosis. When neurological deficit (weakness or numbness) is present alongside pain, specialist evaluation is warranted.

**What to do:** See a neurosurgeon or neurologist within one to two weeks, sooner if symptoms are progressing. If bladder or bowel function is affected, treat as a Red Flag and attend Emergency immediately.

---

### Balance Problems or Difficulty Walking

New or progressive difficulty with balance, coordination, or gait — particularly if not explained by known musculoskeletal disease — may indicate a cerebellar, brainstem, or spinal cord lesion.

**What to do:** Book a specialist consultation. MRI brain and/or spine is typically indicated.

---

### Cognitive Changes

Gradual decline in memory, concentration, word-finding ability, or executive function — particularly if noticed by family members or colleagues — warrants specialist evaluation. While many causes of cognitive change are benign or reversible (including medication side effects, sleep disturbance, depression, and thyroid disease), structural brain pathology must be excluded.

**What to do:** See a specialist. A cognitive assessment and brain MRI are typically part of the workup.

---

### Tinnitus or Hearing Loss on One Side

Unilateral tinnitus (ringing in one ear) or unilateral sensorineural hearing loss — particularly if progressive — should prompt evaluation to exclude an acoustic neuroma (vestibular schwannoma), a benign tumour of the hearing nerve.

**What to do:** Arrange audiological assessment and MRI internal auditory meati.

---

### Facial Numbness or Weakness (Gradual Onset)

Gradually progressive facial numbness or weakness — as opposed to the sudden onset seen in stroke — may indicate a trigeminal or facial nerve lesion, or a skull base pathology. Specialist review and MRI are warranted.

**What to do:** Book a specialist consultation.

---

### New or Unexplained Visual Changes

Double vision, blurred vision, gradual loss of peripheral visual field, or other new visual symptoms — not explained by a refractive error or known eye disease — warrant neurological evaluation to exclude a pituitary tumour, optic pathway lesion, or raised intracranial pressure.

**What to do:** See an ophthalmologist and arrange a neurology or neurosurgery referral as appropriate.

## GREEN FLAG Symptoms — Common, Usually Benign, Worth Monitoring

These symptoms are very common and, in the large majority of cases, are not caused by a serious neurological condition. They can usually be managed conservatively — with lifestyle modification, physiotherapy, or your GP. However, they should be monitored, and if they change in character, worsen, or do not improve with appropriate management, further evaluation may be warranted.

---

### Tension-Type Headache

The most common type of headache — a bilateral, pressing or tightening sensation, typically mild to moderate in intensity, not worsened by routine activity. Often triggered by stress, poor posture, dehydration, caffeine, or disrupted sleep.

**Typical features:** Both sides of the head; pressure or band-like quality; no nausea or vomiting; not worsened by light or noise (or only mildly so).

**What to do:** Address lifestyle triggers. Adequate hydration, regular sleep, posture correction, and stress management are effective first-line measures. Over-the-counter analgesia is appropriate for acute episodes. See your GP if headaches are frequent or not responding to simple measures.

---

### Occasional Non-Specific Back Pain

Back pain without neurological features (no leg weakness, numbness, or bladder/bowel involvement) — particularly if related to posture, activity, or a clear mechanical cause — is very common and usually resolves with conservative management.

**What to do:** Physiotherapy, core strengthening, posture correction, and avoiding prolonged static postures. See your GP if pain is persistent, severe, or not improving. See a specialist if neurological features develop.

---

### Stress-Related Symptoms

Symptoms including fatigue, difficulty concentrating, sleep disturbance, mild memory lapses, palpitations, and non-specific dizziness are frequently related to stress, anxiety, or burnout rather than neurological disease. These are real symptoms with real impact — but they do not typically require a neurosurgical assessment.

**What to do:** Prioritise sleep, stress reduction, and mental health support. See your GP. If symptoms are severe or significantly impairing daily function, psychological assessment may be helpful.

---

### Migraine (Established, Stable Pattern)

If you have a long-established diagnosis of migraine with a stable, recognised pattern — and your current episodes are consistent with that pattern — you do not typically require specialist neurosurgical review. Migraine is managed by neurologists and GPs.

**Important:** If your migraine pattern changes significantly — new aura, prolonged aura, increased frequency, or association with new neurological symptoms — this warrants re-evaluation.

## Questions to Ask Your Doctor

When you see a doctor or specialist about neurological symptoms, consider asking:

- What is the most likely cause of my symptoms?
- What investigations do you recommend, and why?
- Are there any symptoms I should watch for that would require urgent review?
- What is the plan if my symptoms do not improve?
- Is there anything in my lifestyle or activities that might be contributing?
- Do I need to see a specialist, or is GP management appropriate?
- How long should I wait before seeking review if the symptoms persist?

---

## When in Doubt, Seek Assessment

This checklist is a guide — not a diagnostic instrument, and not a replacement for clinical assessment. Neurology is a field where symptoms overlap, where context matters, and where a trained specialist brings judgment that no checklist can replicate.

If you are uncertain about your symptoms, or if something feels wrong that you cannot quite articulate, trust that instinct and seek evaluation. The cost of attending a clinic appointment and being reassured is low. The cost of ignoring a significant symptom can be irreversible.
