## Understanding Headache & Facial Pain Disorders

Headache is the most common neurological symptom encountered in clinical practice. While the vast majority of headaches are primary — meaning they arise from the nervous system itself rather than from underlying structural disease — a significant number of patients suffer from conditions that are misdiagnosed, undertreated, or inadequately managed for years.

As a neurosurgeon with specialist training at the University of Oxford, Dr Nor Faizal brings a structural, neurological, and interventional perspective to headache and facial pain disorders. This is particularly valuable when:

- Headaches are severe, frequent, or disabling
- Standard medications have failed or produced intolerable side effects
- Facial pain is present alongside or instead of headache
- A structural cause needs to be excluded
- Interventional or surgical treatment may be appropriate

## Chronic Headache

### What Is Chronic Headache?

A headache disorder is classified as **chronic** when headaches occur on **15 or more days per month** for more than three months. Chronic headache is a significant public health problem — it impairs work performance, disrupts relationships, and profoundly affects quality of life.

Chronic headache encompasses several subtypes:

### Chronic Tension-Type Headache (CTTH)
The most prevalent form of chronic headache. Characterised by a bilateral, pressing or tightening sensation — often described as a "band around the head" — that is typically mild to moderate in intensity and does not worsen with physical activity.

- **Duration:** 30 minutes to several days
- **Features:** No nausea, no photophobia/phonophobia (or only one of these)
- **Triggers:** Stress, poor posture, disrupted sleep, muscle tension
- **Management:** Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline), physiotherapy, biofeedback, behavioural strategies

### Medication Overuse Headache (MOH)
One of the most common and underrecognised causes of chronic daily headache. MOH — also called *analgesic rebound headache* — develops when pain-relieving medications are used too frequently (typically ≥10–15 days per month, depending on the agent).

Almost any headache medication can cause MOH if overused: paracetamol, NSAIDs, triptans, codeine-containing preparations, and combination analgesics. The result is a paradoxical increase in headache frequency — the medication that once provided relief begins to sustain the pain cycle.

**Breaking the cycle requires:**
1. Gradual or supervised withdrawal of the overused medication
2. Prophylactic therapy to prevent headache recurrence
3. Patient education on appropriate medication use limits

### New Daily Persistent Headache (NDPH)
A form of chronic headache that begins suddenly — often on a precise, memorable day — and becomes continuous from onset. Unlike tension-type headache, which typically evolves gradually, NDPH is distinguished by its abrupt, unremitting onset.

NDPH can be difficult to treat and may represent a post-viral or neuroimmune phenomenon in some cases. Investigation is warranted to exclude secondary causes.

### Secondary Causes of Chronic Headache
Not all chronic headaches are primary. A thorough neurological evaluation is essential to exclude:

- **Idiopathic intracranial hypertension (IIH)** — raised CSF pressure without a structural lesion; associated with visual symptoms and papilloedema
- **Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) leak** — low-pressure headache characterised by positional worsening (worse upright, better lying flat)
- **Venous sinus thrombosis** — thrombosis of the dural venous sinuses, presenting as progressive headache with possible neurological signs
- **Skull base lesions and meningiomas** — slow-growing tumours may present purely as headache for months
- **Chiari malformation** — hindbrain herniation causing occipital headaches worsened by Valsalva manoeuvres
- **Cervicogenic headache** — headache originating from the cervical spine, often unilateral, exacerbated by neck movement

## Migraine

### The Neurobiology of Migraine

Migraine is far more than a severe headache. It is a complex neurological disorder involving cortical spreading depression, trigeminovascular activation, and altered pain processing throughout the central nervous system.

Affecting approximately 12–15% of the population globally, migraine is one of the leading causes of years lived with disability worldwide — ranking third among all diseases for global burden of disease in working-age adults.

### Classification

**Migraine Without Aura (Common Migraine)**
The most prevalent form. Characterised by:
- Unilateral, pulsating headache
- Moderate to severe intensity
- Worsened by routine physical activity
- Associated with nausea and/or vomiting, photophobia, and phonophobia
- Duration: 4–72 hours untreated

**Migraine With Aura (Classic Migraine)**
Approximately one-third of migraine sufferers experience aura — a set of focal neurological symptoms that typically precede or accompany the headache:

| Aura Type | Symptoms | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Visual | Scintillating scotoma, zigzag fortification spectra, visual field defects | 5–60 minutes |
| Sensory | Pins and needles or numbness spreading across the face or hand | 5–60 minutes |
| Language | Dysphasia or speech difficulty | 5–30 minutes |
| Motor | Weakness (rare — hemiplegic migraine) | Up to 72 hours |

**Chronic Migraine**
When migraine headaches occur on ≥15 days per month (with ≥8 days fulfilling migraine criteria), the diagnosis becomes chronic migraine. This represents the most disabling end of the migraine spectrum and often requires specialist management.

**Vestibular Migraine**
An increasingly recognised variant in which migraine is associated with vestibular symptoms — vertigo, dizziness, imbalance, or motion sensitivity — rather than or in addition to headache. Vestibular migraine accounts for a significant proportion of recurrent vertigo in adults.

### The Migraine Prodrome and Postdrome

Migraine does not begin and end with the headache. The full attack comprises four phases:

1. **Prodrome (hours to days before):** Yawning, food cravings, mood changes, neck stiffness, cognitive fog
2. **Aura (if present, 5–60 minutes):** Neurological symptoms as above
3. **Headache phase (4–72 hours):** Characteristic unilateral, pulsating pain with sensory sensitivities
4. **Postdrome (24–48 hours after):** Cognitive slowness, fatigue, head tenderness — the "migraine hangover"

### Migraine Triggers

Common identifiable triggers include:
- **Hormonal fluctuations** — particularly perimenstrual oestrogen withdrawal in women
- **Sleep disruption** — both insufficient and excessive sleep
- **Dietary factors** — alcohol (especially red wine and beer), caffeine fluctuation, prolonged fasting
- **Sensory stimuli** — bright lights, strong odours, loud sounds
- **Weather changes** — barometric pressure changes
- **Psychological stress** — particularly the "let-down" after stress
- **Medications** — vasodilators (nitrates), oral contraceptive pills in susceptible individuals

### Acute Migraine Treatment

**Step 1 — Simple Analgesia:** Aspirin 900 mg, ibuprofen 400–600 mg, or paracetamol 1000 mg, taken early in the attack with an antiemetic if needed.

**Step 2 — Triptans:** Serotonin 5-HT1B/1D receptor agonists are the most effective class of acute migraine treatment available. Sumatriptan, rizatriptan, zolmitriptan, and others are available. Triptans are most effective when taken early and should not be used more than 10 days per month.

**Step 3 — CGRP antagonists (gepants):** A newer class of acute migraine treatment. Unlike triptans, gepants do not carry a risk of medication overuse headache with frequent use.

**Dihydroergotamine (DHE):** Particularly useful for prolonged attacks and status migrainosus (migraine lasting >72 hours).

### Preventive Migraine Treatment

When attacks are frequent (≥4 days/month), severe, or disabling, preventive therapy should be discussed:

| Category | Agents | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Beta-blockers | Propranolol, metoprolol | First-line; avoid in asthma |
| Anticonvulsants | Topiramate, valproate | Effective; teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy) |
| Antidepressants | Amitriptyline, venlafaxine | Useful when comorbid depression/anxiety |
| Calcium channel blockers | Flunarizine | Well-tolerated; weight gain common |
| CGRP monoclonal antibodies | Erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab | Monthly/quarterly injection; highly effective for chronic migraine |

**OnabotulinumtoxinA (Botox):** The PREEMPT protocol — 31 injections across head and neck musculature every 12 weeks — is an established preventive treatment for chronic migraine. It is particularly effective when muscle trigger points contribute to headache.

### Interventional Approaches for Refractory Migraine

When medication fails or is not tolerated, interventional options include:

**Greater Occipital Nerve Block:** Injection of local anaesthetic and corticosteroid around the greater occipital nerve at the base of the skull. Provides rapid relief for occipital headache and can break a prolonged migraine cycle.

**Sphenopalatine Ganglion Block (SPG Block):** The SPG is a parasympathetic ganglion located behind the nose, closely associated with migraine pathophysiology. Transnasal SPG block is a minimally invasive procedure that can abort acute attacks and reduce headache frequency.

**Occipital Nerve Stimulation:** For highly refractory chronic migraine, implanted neurostimulators placed along the occipital nerves can provide significant long-term relief.

## Trigeminal Neuralgia

### What Is Trigeminal Neuralgia?

Trigeminal neuralgia (TN) — historically known as *tic douloureux* — is widely regarded as one of the most painful conditions known to medicine. It causes sudden, severe, electric shock-like facial pain in the distribution of one or more branches of the trigeminal nerve (the 5th cranial nerve).

The pain is typically:
- **Unilateral** (one side of the face)
- **Brief** — lasting a fraction of a second to 2 minutes per episode
- **Excruciating** — often described as the worst pain imaginable
- **Triggered** by minimal stimuli: talking, eating, drinking, brushing teeth, a light touch to the face, or a cold breeze

### The Trigeminal Nerve and Its Divisions

The trigeminal nerve has three branches (divisions):

| Division | Territory | Most Commonly Affected in TN |
|---|---|---|
| V1 (Ophthalmic) | Forehead, eye, upper nose | Least common |
| V2 (Maxillary) | Cheek, upper lip, upper teeth, palate | Common |
| V3 (Mandibular) | Lower lip, lower teeth, jaw, tongue | Most common (V2+V3) |

The majority of TN affects the V2 and V3 distributions — pain in the cheek, jaw, teeth, and lower face.

### Classification

**Classical Trigeminal Neuralgia (Type 1 / CTN)**
Caused by vascular compression of the trigeminal nerve root at its entry zone into the brainstem — typically by the superior cerebellar artery or anterior inferior cerebellar artery. The pulsating vessel compresses and gradually demyelinates the nerve root, generating spontaneous ectopic impulses and abnormal pain signalling.

This is confirmed on high-resolution MRI with FIESTA/CISS sequences showing the offending vessel in contact with the nerve root.

**Secondary Trigeminal Neuralgia**
Caused by an identifiable structural lesion affecting the trigeminal pathway:
- Multiple sclerosis (MS) — demyelinating plaque at the trigeminal root entry zone
- Skull base tumours — meningioma, epidermoid cyst, schwannoma
- Vascular malformations
- Post-herpetic neuralgia (following herpes zoster affecting V1)

Secondary TN typically occurs in younger patients and may present bilaterally. A thorough investigation is mandatory.

**Idiopathic Trigeminal Neuralgia (Type 2)**
When no vascular compression or structural lesion is identified. This is a diagnosis of exclusion.

### Diagnosis

Diagnosis is primarily clinical. Key features that distinguish TN from other facial pain conditions:

| Feature | Trigeminal Neuralgia | Atypical Facial Pain |
|---|---|---|
| Character | Electric, stabbing, lancinating | Aching, burning, dull |
| Duration | Seconds to 2 minutes | Hours to constant |
| Triggers | Specific light touch triggers | Variable, often no triggers |
| Distribution | Strict trigeminal territory | May not follow dermatomal pattern |
| Between attacks | Pain-free intervals | Background pain often present |

**Investigations:**
- **MRI brain with FIESTA/CISS sequences** — to identify neurovascular conflict and exclude secondary causes
- **Neurophysiological studies** (trigeminal reflex testing) — in selected cases

### Medical Management

Carbamazepine (an anticonvulsant) is the first-line treatment for TN, with a response rate of 70–80% initially. The mechanism is sodium channel blockade, suppressing the ectopic neuronal discharge within the nerve.

| Medication | Evidence | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Carbamazepine | First-line (Level A) | Monitor LFTs, blood count; drug interactions common |
| Oxcarbazepine | Alternative first-line | Better tolerability than carbamazepine |
| Lamotrigine | Second-line add-on | Slow titration required |
| Baclofen | Adjunct | Useful with carbamazepine; GABA-B agonist |
| Gabapentin/Pregabalin | Adjunct | Particularly for atypical features |

Unfortunately, carbamazepine loses efficacy in many patients over time, side effects (dizziness, hyponatraemia, cognitive slowing) limit dose escalation, and drug interactions create management complexity. When medication fails or is not tolerated, surgical treatment becomes the best option.

### Surgical Treatment

Surgery is highly effective for classical TN and should be considered when:
- Medications fail to control pain adequately
- Side effects of medications are intolerable
- Quality of life is severely affected
- The patient has classical TN with MRI-confirmed neurovascular conflict

**Microvascular Decompression (MVD)**

MVD is the definitive surgical treatment for classical TN. It is the only procedure that addresses the underlying cause — vascular compression — rather than simply damaging the nerve.

**Procedure:** A small craniotomy is made behind the ear. Under the operating microscope, the offending blood vessel is identified in contact with the trigeminal nerve root. The vessel is carefully mobilised away from the nerve, and a small Teflon implant is placed to prevent re-contact.

**Outcomes:**
- Immediate pain relief in >90% of patients
- Long-term pain freedom at 5 years: 70–80%
- Recurrence rate at 10 years: approximately 25–30%
- Preservation of facial sensation

MVD is particularly recommended for younger patients, those with typical TN features, and when imaging confirms neurovascular conflict.

**Stereotactic Radiosurgery (Gamma Knife / CyberKnife)**

A non-invasive outpatient procedure in which a focused beam of radiation is delivered precisely to the trigeminal nerve root. No incision or anaesthesia is required.

- **Effect:** Gradual over 1–3 months
- **Pain relief:** 70–85% initially; durable in 50–60% at 5 years
- **Risk:** Facial numbness in 30–50% of cases; rarely dysaesthesia
- **Best for:** Elderly patients, those unfit for surgery, or when MVD is not feasible

**Percutaneous Ablative Procedures**

Performed through a needle inserted through the cheek into the trigeminal ganglion (Gasserian ganglion) under X-ray guidance:

- **Balloon microcompression:** A balloon is inflated against the ganglion, selectively damaging large fibres; useful for V1 involvement and the elderly
- **Glycerol rhizolysis:** Glycerol is injected into the trigeminal cistern to damage pain fibres selectively
- **Radiofrequency thermocoagulation:** Controlled heat lesion of the ganglion; allows intraoperative testing to tailor the degree of numbness

All percutaneous procedures carry a higher recurrence rate than MVD but involve shorter hospital stays and can be repeated.

## Occipital Neuralgia & Other Facial Pain Syndromes

### Occipital Neuralgia
Sharp, shooting pain in the distribution of the greater or lesser occipital nerves — from the base of the skull to the scalp and behind the eye. Often confused with migraine but distinguished by:
- Localised tenderness over the nerve at the occiput
- Pain triggered by pressure on the nerve
- Response to diagnostic occipital nerve block

**Treatment:** Nerve block, physiotherapy, radiofrequency ablation of the nerve, or occipital nerve stimulation for refractory cases.

### Cluster Headache
A primary headache disorder characterised by strictly unilateral, severe periorbital or temporal pain associated with ipsilateral autonomic features: lacrimation, rhinorrhoea, ptosis, miosis, conjunctival injection, and eyelid oedema. Attacks occur in clusters — often for weeks — then remit completely.

Cluster headache is frequently underdiagnosed and misdiagnosed as sinusitis or dental pain. It responds to specific treatments including high-flow oxygen, subcutaneous sumatriptan, and verapamil for prevention.

### Hemicrania Continua
A continuous, strictly unilateral headache with superimposed exacerbations and associated autonomic features. It is uniquely defined by its **absolute and complete response to indomethacin** — a finding that is both diagnostic and therapeutic.

### Short-lasting Unilateral Neuralgiform Headache Attacks (SUNHA/SUNA/SUNCT)
Extremely brief (seconds), very frequent attacks of severe unilateral headache with cranial autonomic features. Distinguished from TN by the conjunctival injection and tearing that accompany attacks. Often resistant to treatment; lamotrigine and gabapentin may help.

## Red Flag Headache Symptoms

Not all headaches are benign. The following features require urgent neurological evaluation to exclude a serious secondary cause:

| Red Flag | Possible Cause |
|---|---|
| Thunderclap headache — instantaneous onset ("worst headache of my life") | Subarachnoid haemorrhage |
| New headache in patient >50 years | Temporal arteritis, malignancy, or vascular disease |
| Progressively worsening headache over weeks | Raised intracranial pressure, mass lesion |
| Headache with fever and neck stiffness | Meningitis, encephalitis |
| Headache with neurological signs (weakness, visual loss, confusion) | Intracranial mass, stroke, venous thrombosis |
| Headache worse on lying down or with Valsalva | Raised intracranial pressure |
| Positional headache (worse upright, better flat) | CSF leak |
| New headache in immunocompromised patient | CNS infection, lymphoma |
| Headache following head injury | Intracranial haemorrhage, CSF leak |

**If you experience a sudden, severe headache of instantaneous onset, treat it as a medical emergency. Go directly to the emergency department.**

## When to See a Specialist

You should seek specialist evaluation if:

- Headaches occur more than 4 days per month or are severely disabling
- Your headaches have changed in character, frequency, or severity
- Over-the-counter medications no longer provide adequate relief
- You are taking pain medications more than 10 days per month
- You experience facial pain, numbness, or electric-shock sensations
- You have associated neurological symptoms (vision changes, weakness, numbness)
- Headaches interfere significantly with work, study, or daily life
- Previous specialist treatment has been insufficient
