# Emergency Neurosurgery

Some neurological events do not allow time for scheduled appointments or second opinions. When the brain or spinal cord is under acute threat — from trauma, haemorrhage, or rapidly progressing compression — every hour matters. Emergency neurosurgery is the discipline of acting decisively, under pressure, to save life and preserve neurological function.

## What Constitutes a Neurosurgical Emergency?

A neurosurgical emergency is any condition involving the brain, spinal cord, or peripheral nervous system that poses an immediate threat to life or neurological function and requires urgent — often surgical — intervention. These conditions can deteriorate within minutes to hours, and delay in treatment is directly correlated with worse outcomes.

The principal neurosurgical emergencies include:

- Traumatic brain injury (TBI) with intracranial haemorrhage or raised intracranial pressure
- Acute spinal cord injury with neurological compromise
- Spontaneous intracranial haemorrhage (subarachnoid, intracerebral, or intraventricular)
- Acute hydrocephalus
- Malignant spinal cord compression
- Brain abscess with mass effect
- Depressed skull fractures with underlying brain injury
- Shunt malfunction causing acute raised intracranial pressure

## Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

### Classification

TBI is classified by severity using the **Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)**:

| Severity | GCS Score | Features |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | 13–15 | Brief or no loss of consciousness; may have post-traumatic amnesia |
| Moderate | 9–12 | Loss of consciousness; confusion; localising neurological signs |
| Severe | 3–8 | Prolonged unconsciousness; significant neurological deficit |

Even mild TBI warrants careful observation, as a small proportion of patients deteriorate due to evolving intracranial pathology — a phenomenon known as "talk and die."

### Common TBI Lesions Requiring Surgery

**Extradural Haematoma (EDH)**
Arterial bleeding — typically from the middle meningeal artery following temporal skull fracture — accumulates between the skull and dura mater. EDH classically presents with a "lucid interval": the patient loses consciousness, recovers briefly, then deteriorates rapidly. This is a surgical emergency. Evacuation of the clot is life-saving and outcomes are excellent when surgery is performed promptly.

**Acute Subdural Haematoma (SDH)**
Venous bleeding below the dura, typically from torn bridging veins. Acute SDH in the context of significant TBI carries high mortality and morbidity. Surgical evacuation (craniotomy) is indicated when clot thickness exceeds 10 mm, midline shift exceeds 5 mm, or when neurological deterioration occurs.

**Intracerebral Haematoma (ICH) / Contusion**
Bleeding within the brain parenchyma resulting from direct brain injury. Surgical evacuation is considered when haematoma size is large, located in an accessible region, and associated with neurological deterioration or mass effect.

**Depressed Skull Fracture**
When bone fragments are displaced inward by more than the thickness of the skull table, surgical elevation is required to prevent ongoing brain compression, dural laceration, and infection risk — particularly when the fracture overlies a major dural venous sinus or is open (compound).

### Managing Raised Intracranial Pressure
In severe TBI, the primary threat is often raised intracranial pressure (ICP) causing secondary brain injury. Surgical options include placement of an ICP monitor, external ventricular drainage (EVD), and in selected cases, decompressive craniectomy — removal of a large bone flap to allow the swollen brain to expand without fatal compression.

## Acute Spinal Cord Injury

### Classification

Spinal cord injuries are classified using the **ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS)**:

| Grade | Description |
|---|---|
| AIS A | Complete — no motor or sensory function below the injury level |
| AIS B | Incomplete — sensory but no motor function below injury level |
| AIS C | Incomplete — motor function present but less than half of muscles grade ≥3 |
| AIS D | Incomplete — majority of key muscles grade ≥3 |
| AIS E | Normal motor and sensory function |

Incomplete injuries (AIS B–D) carry substantially greater potential for neurological recovery, making early decompression and stabilisation critically important.

### Management and Surgical Timing

Early management of acute spinal cord injury involves:

1. **Immobilisation and spinal precautions** — preventing further injury during transport and assessment
2. **Haemodynamic optimisation** — maintaining mean arterial pressure above 85 mmHg to perfuse the injured cord
3. **Imaging** — urgent MRI to assess cord compression, ligamentous injury, and fracture pattern
4. **Surgical decompression and stabilisation** — the evidence strongly supports surgery within 24 hours for incomplete injuries, with growing evidence that ultra-early surgery (within 8 hours) improves neurological recovery

Surgical techniques include posterior laminectomy with instrumented fusion, anterior cervical discectomy and fusion, and corpectomy with cage reconstruction — chosen based on fracture pattern, level, and mechanism of injury.

## Spontaneous Intracranial Haemorrhage

### Subarachnoid Haemorrhage (SAH)

Subarachnoid haemorrhage — bleeding into the space surrounding the brain — most commonly results from rupture of an intracranial aneurysm. The hallmark presentation is the **thunderclap headache**: a severe headache of instantaneous onset, often described as "the worst headache of my life."

SAH is a life-threatening emergency with a 30-day mortality of approximately 40–50% when untreated. Emergency management involves:

- CT brain to confirm haemorrhage
- CT angiography or catheter angiography to identify the aneurysm
- Surgical clipping or endovascular coiling of the aneurysm to prevent re-bleeding
- Management of vasospasm, hydrocephalus, and systemic complications in the neurological intensive care unit

### Intracerebral Haemorrhage (ICH)

Spontaneous bleeding into the brain parenchyma is most commonly caused by hypertension, amyloid angiopathy, arteriovenous malformation, or anticoagulant use. Surgical evacuation is considered for:
- Large lobar haematomas with mass effect and neurological deterioration
- Cerebellar haematomas greater than 3 cm, which can rapidly cause brainstem compression and death
- Haematomas from underlying vascular lesions amenable to treatment

### Intraventricular Haemorrhage (IVH)

Blood within the ventricular system causes acute obstructive hydrocephalus. Emergency EVD insertion allows drainage of blood-stained CSF, control of ICP, and intrathecal thrombolytic therapy to clear the clot.

## Hydrocephalus Emergencies

Acute hydrocephalus — abnormal accumulation of cerebrospinal fluid causing dangerous rise in intracranial pressure — can result from haemorrhage, tumour obstruction, infection, or shunt failure. Symptoms include severe headache, vomiting, drowsiness, and visual changes. If untreated, it rapidly causes herniation and death.

**External Ventricular Drain (EVD)**: A catheter inserted into the ventricular system through a small burr hole, allowing continuous drainage of CSF and ICP monitoring. This is a bedside or theatre procedure that can be lifesaving within minutes.

**Shunt Malfunction**: Patients with pre-existing hydrocephalus managed by a ventriculoperitoneal (VP) or ventriculoatrial (VA) shunt can present in crisis when the shunt fails. Revision surgery — replacing the blocked or over-draining component — is typically required urgently.

## Malignant Spinal Cord Compression

When cancer spreads to the vertebral column and compresses the spinal cord, it constitutes an **oncological emergency**. This condition can convert a mobile, independent patient into someone who is paraplegic and incontinent within hours to days.

**Immediate treatment** involves high-dose corticosteroids (dexamethasone) to reduce inflammatory oedema around the cord, followed by urgent imaging and specialist evaluation. Surgical decompression — typically posterior laminectomy with spinal stabilisation — followed by stereotactic radiosurgery or radiotherapy, offers the best chance of preserving or restoring neurological function.

The window of opportunity is narrow: patients who are treated before complete neurological loss are far more likely to regain or maintain function than those treated after paraplegia is established.

## Warning Signs Requiring Immediate Emergency Evaluation

**Go directly to the emergency department or call emergency services if you or someone nearby experiences:**

- Sudden severe headache — instantaneous onset, "worst ever"
- Loss of consciousness or unresponsiveness
- Sudden weakness or paralysis of one side of the body
- Sudden inability to speak or understand speech
- Sudden vision loss in one or both eyes
- Neck stiffness with headache and fever (signs of meningitis)
- Sudden loss of bladder or bowel control with back pain
- Rapidly progressive weakness of both legs
- Head or spine injury following significant trauma
- Seizures in someone with no prior epilepsy diagnosis
- Confusion, drowsiness, or altered behaviour following a head injury

**Do not wait for these symptoms to improve on their own. Time lost is brain and spinal cord lost.**

## First Aid for Head Injuries

If someone sustains a head injury, follow these principles while waiting for emergency services:

1. **Call emergency services** (999 in Malaysia) for any significant head injury
2. **Keep the person still** — do not move them unnecessarily; assume a spine injury until proven otherwise
3. **Do not remove a helmet** if one is worn — unless the person is not breathing
4. **Maintain the airway** — if unconscious but breathing, place in the recovery position carefully, supporting the head and neck
5. **Control external bleeding** — apply gentle pressure to scalp wounds with a clean cloth; do not apply pressure if you suspect a skull fracture beneath
6. **Do not give anything to eat or drink** — surgery may be needed urgently
7. **Monitor consciousness** — talk to the person, note if they become increasingly drowsy or confused
8. **Note the mechanism of injury** — speed of vehicle, height of fall, whether consciousness was lost — this information is critical for the treating team

## What Happens in Emergency Neurosurgery

Understanding the process can reduce anxiety for families waiting outside the operating theatre.

**1. Assessment and stabilisation (Emergency Department)**
On arrival, the resuscitation team secures the airway, stabilises vital signs, and performs emergency imaging. A neurosurgical assessment is conducted immediately for any patient with neurological symptoms.

**2. Imaging review**
CT brain and/or CT spine is performed within minutes. If haemorrhage, mass effect, or cord compression is identified, the neurosurgeon is contacted immediately.

**3. Decision and consent**
The neurosurgeon discusses the findings and recommended procedure with the patient — if conscious — or with the next of kin. In immediately life-threatening situations, emergency surgery proceeds on a life-saving basis.

**4. Surgery**
Depending on the condition, surgery may involve evacuation of a haematoma, repair of a fractured spine, insertion of an EVD, or clipping of a ruptured aneurysm. Most emergency neurosurgical procedures take between 1 and 4 hours.

**5. Intensive Care Unit (ICU) / High Dependency Unit (HDU)**
Following surgery, patients are monitored in an intensive care setting where neurological observations, ICP monitoring, and haemodynamic support are maintained.

**6. Rehabilitation**
As the patient stabilises, a multidisciplinary rehabilitation programme — involving physiotherapy, speech therapy, occupational therapy, and neuropsychology — begins. Recovery from acute neurological injury is a process measured in weeks to months, not days.

## Prognosis and Recovery

Outcomes from emergency neurosurgery vary considerably by diagnosis, severity, and timing of treatment. Several principles hold consistently across conditions:

- **Earlier intervention is better** — the neurological insult of delay is often irreversible
- **Incomplete deficits recover better than complete ones** — preserving residual function is the priority
- **Young patients and those without major co-morbidities** recover more fully
- **Rehabilitation accelerates and maximises recovery** — do not underestimate the role of structured neurological rehabilitation

Modern emergency neurosurgery, combined with neurocritical care and rehabilitation, has significantly improved survival and functional outcomes over the past two decades. Many patients who would not have survived 20 years ago now return to independent lives.
