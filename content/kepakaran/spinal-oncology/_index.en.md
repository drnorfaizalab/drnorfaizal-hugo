# Spinal Oncology Surgery

Spinal tumours — whether arising primarily within the spine or spreading from cancer elsewhere in the body — represent some of the most technically demanding and consequential conditions in neurosurgery. The spine houses the spinal cord and nerve roots, meaning that tumour growth can threaten not only life but also neurological function, independence, and quality of life.

Dr Nor Faizal has specialist training in spinal oncology surgery, offering patients and their oncology teams a dedicated neurosurgical partner for the management of spinal tumours. The approach is precision-focused: preserving neurological function, maintaining spinal stability, enabling radiotherapy, and — where appropriate — achieving cure.

---

## What is Spinal Oncology?

Spinal oncology is the subspecialty concerned with the diagnosis and surgical management of tumours affecting the spine — including the vertebral bones, epidural space, spinal cord, and nerve roots. It sits at the intersection of oncology, neurosurgery, and spinal surgery, requiring expertise in all three domains.

The goals of spinal oncology surgery depend on the nature and extent of disease:

- **For primary spinal tumours**: achieve the maximum safe resection with curative intent where possible
- **For metastatic disease**: decompress the spinal cord, stabilise the spine, enable adjuvant radiotherapy, and restore or preserve neurological function and quality of life
- **For all patients**: minimise surgical morbidity and support the broader oncological treatment plan

## Types of Spinal Tumours

### Primary Spinal Tumours

Primary spinal tumours originate from the cells of the spine and spinal cord itself. They are relatively rare compared to metastatic disease.

**Intramedullary tumours** (within the spinal cord tissue):
- **Ependymoma** — the most common intramedullary tumour in adults, arising from ependymal cells lining the central canal. Often well-defined, with a good plane for surgical resection.
- **Astrocytoma** — arises from astrocytes within the cord, typically more infiltrative and presenting a greater surgical challenge.
- **Hemangioblastoma** — highly vascular tumour, associated with von Hippel-Lindau syndrome, technically challenging due to its blood supply.

**Intradural extramedullary tumours** (within the dural sac but outside the cord):
- **Meningioma** — arises from the meninges; usually benign and surgically curable.
- **Schwannoma and neurofibroma** — arise from nerve sheath cells; often associated with specific nerve roots.

**Extradural tumours** (outside the dural sac, within or adjacent to vertebral bone):
- **Chordoma** — arises from remnants of the notochord, typically at the sacrum or clivus; locally aggressive with high recurrence rates.
- **Giant cell tumour, osteoblastoma, aneurysmal bone cyst** — primary bone tumours of varying aggressiveness.

### Metastatic Spinal Tumours

Metastatic disease accounts for the majority of spinal tumours seen in clinical practice. The spine is the most common site of skeletal metastasis. Common primary cancers that spread to the spine include:

| Primary Cancer | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| Lung | Often aggressive, rapid growth; may present acutely |
| Breast | Osteolytic or osteoblastic; may be hormone-sensitive |
| Prostate | Predominantly osteoblastic; often multi-level |
| Kidney (Renal cell) | Highly vascular; risk of significant intraoperative bleeding |
| Thyroid | Well-differentiated; long natural history even with metastases |
| Multiple myeloma | Diffuse vertebral involvement; pathological fractures common |

## Understanding Spinal Tumour Anatomy

The location of a tumour within the spine determines its symptoms, surgical approach, and prognosis.

**By spinal level**: Cervical, thoracic, lumbar, and sacral locations each carry different risks and require different surgical strategies.

**By compartment**:
- **Extradural**: Outside the dura mater — typically metastatic or primary bone tumours
- **Intradural extramedullary**: Within the dura but outside the cord — typically nerve sheath tumours and meningiomas
- **Intramedullary**: Within the substance of the spinal cord — typically primary glial tumours

This anatomical classification directly guides surgical planning and informs the expected difficulty of resection.

## Symptoms of Spinal Tumours

Spinal tumours can present with a range of symptoms, from subtle to catastrophic. Awareness of these patterns allows for earlier diagnosis and better outcomes.

**Axial pain**: The most common early symptom. Unlike mechanical back pain, tumour-related pain is often constant, progressive, and worse at night or at rest — not relieved by lying down.

**Radiculopathy**: Pain, numbness, or weakness radiating along a nerve root distribution — similar to sciatica but often bilateral or not clearly related to position or movement.

**Myelopathy**: Spinal cord dysfunction producing weakness in the limbs, spasticity, altered gait, or a sensation of heaviness or incoordination. This is a neurological emergency when rapidly progressive.

**Bowel and bladder dysfunction**: Loss of bladder control, urinary retention, or bowel incontinence indicates compression of the conus medullaris or cauda equina — requiring urgent surgical evaluation.

**Neurological deficit**: Partial or complete weakness in the legs (paraparesis/paraplegia) or all four limbs (tetraparesis/tetraplegia) represents advanced cord compression.

## Diagnostic Workup

Accurate diagnosis before surgery is essential and typically involves:

- **MRI spine with gadolinium contrast**: The gold standard imaging modality. Delineates tumour extent, cord compression, dural involvement, and relationship to nerve structures.
- **CT spine**: Provides bone detail for surgical planning and assessment of vertebral destruction and stability.
- **CT chest/abdomen/pelvis or PET-CT**: Identifies or stages the primary cancer and evaluates systemic disease burden.
- **Bone scan or SPECT-CT**: Useful for detecting multi-level skeletal involvement.
- **CT-guided biopsy**: For histological diagnosis when the primary is unknown or when tissue confirmation is required before treatment.
- **Angiography and embolisation**: For highly vascular tumours (renal cell, thyroid) prior to surgery to reduce intraoperative bleeding.

## Surgical Approaches

### Posterior Decompression and Stabilisation
The most commonly performed approach for metastatic spinal disease. The surgeon accesses the spine from the back, removes tumour compressing the spinal cord (laminectomy or corpectomy), and stabilises the spine with pedicle screws and rods. This reliably decompresses the cord and allows immediate spinal stability.

### Anterior and Lateral Approaches
For tumours primarily affecting the vertebral body, anterior or lateral approaches allow direct access for corpectomy (removal of the vertebral body) and reconstruction with an expandable cage, followed by posterior stabilisation.

### Minimally Invasive Techniques
Percutaneous pedicle screw fixation, tubular retractor-based decompression, and endoscopic approaches reduce blood loss, operative time, and recovery — particularly important in patients whose medical condition or oncological treatment schedule cannot accommodate prolonged recovery.

### En Bloc Resection
For selected primary spinal tumours — particularly chordoma, chondrosarcoma, and giant cell tumour — en bloc resection (removal of the tumour as a single specimen with clear margins) offers the best chance of cure or long-term local control. This is highly complex surgery requiring meticulous planning using 3D reconstructions and navigation.

### Separation Surgery
A paradigm shift in the management of metastatic spinal cord compression. Rather than attempting complete tumour removal (which carries high morbidity), separation surgery creates a 2–3 mm circumferential margin between the tumour and the spinal cord, converting an inoperable radiotherapy target into one that can be safely treated with stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS). This combination — surgery followed by SRS — provides durable local control with a much smaller operation.

## Spinal Stability Assessment

Tumour destruction of the vertebral column can render the spine unstable, causing pain on movement, progressive deformity, and risk of further neurological injury. The **Spinal Instability Neoplastic Score (SINS)** is a validated clinical tool used to categorise tumour-related spinal instability:

| SINS Score | Stability Category | Surgical Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| 0–6 | Stable | No surgical stabilisation needed |
| 7–12 | Potentially unstable | Surgical consultation warranted |
| 13–18 | Unstable | Surgical stabilisation recommended |

Dr Nor Faizal applies the SINS score in every spinal oncology consultation to determine whether stabilisation is required and to guide the urgency of intervention.

## Adjuvant Treatments

Surgery is almost never the final treatment in spinal oncology — it is part of a multidisciplinary strategy.

**Conventional Radiotherapy**: Used after surgery to treat residual disease or as primary treatment for radiosensitive tumours (lymphoma, myeloma, some metastases).

**Stereotactic Radiosurgery (SRS / CyberKnife / Gamma Knife)**: Highly conformal radiation delivered in 1–5 sessions with sub-millimetre precision. Particularly effective for radioresistant tumours (renal cell carcinoma, melanoma, sarcoma) and following separation surgery.

**Chemotherapy and Targeted Therapy**: Systemic treatment for haematological malignancies and solid tumours with available targeted agents (e.g., tyrosine kinase inhibitors for renal cell carcinoma, BRAF inhibitors for melanoma).

**Hormone Therapy**: For hormone-sensitive primary cancers such as prostate and breast cancer, hormonal manipulation is an important systemic treatment for spinal metastases.

**Bone-Modifying Agents**: Bisphosphonates and denosumab reduce skeletal-related events and may slow the growth of osteolytic metastases.

## Rehabilitation and Prognosis

Neurological recovery after spinal cord decompression depends on the severity and duration of deficit before surgery. The greatest gains are achieved when surgery is performed before complete neurological loss occurs — highlighting the importance of early specialist evaluation.

Postoperative rehabilitation — including physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and pain management — is an integral part of care. Many patients regain functional independence sufficient to resume oncological treatment and maintain quality of life.

Prognosis in spinal oncology is determined by the primary tumour biology, extent of systemic disease, degree of neurological impairment, and spinal stability. These factors are assessed using validated scoring systems such as the **Tokuhashi score** and **Tomita score** to guide surgical decision-making in metastatic disease.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can a spinal tumour be completely cured?**
A: For primary benign tumours — such as meningioma and schwannoma — surgical removal is often curative. For primary malignant tumours such as chordoma, en bloc resection with clear margins offers the best long-term control, though recurrence remains possible. For metastatic disease, the goal shifts from cure to durable local control, neurological preservation, and quality of life — all of which are achievable in many patients.

**Q: How urgent is surgery for spinal cord compression?**
A: Very urgent. When spinal cord compression produces neurological deficit — particularly weakness, bladder dysfunction, or rapidly progressing symptoms — surgery within 24–48 hours significantly improves the chance of neurological recovery. Delays beyond this window reduce the likelihood of functional recovery. If you or a family member have cancer and develop new back pain with any neurological symptoms, seek specialist evaluation the same day.

**Q: Will surgery make my cancer worse by "spreading" the tumour?**
A: This is a common concern but is not supported by evidence. Appropriately planned spinal surgery does not accelerate systemic cancer spread. In fact, surgery enables the continuation of systemic oncological treatments by restoring mobility, reducing pain, and allowing the commencement or continuation of radiotherapy.

**Q: Is minimally invasive surgery always an option?**
A: Minimally invasive approaches are preferred where technically feasible and where the extent of tumour involvement allows. However, some situations — particularly large tumours causing severe cord compression — require more extensive open surgery to achieve adequate decompression. The surgical approach is always chosen to maximise safety and effectiveness, not simply to minimise incision size.

**Q: How do I know if my back pain is from a tumour?**
A: Tumour-related back pain tends to be constant, progressive, and present at rest and at night — features that distinguish it from common mechanical back pain which typically improves with rest. A new onset of these pain characteristics, particularly in someone with a known cancer history, warrants urgent MRI evaluation.

**Q: Can spinal surgery be performed if I am undergoing chemotherapy?**
A: This is carefully evaluated on an individual basis. Timing of surgery relative to chemotherapy cycles, blood counts, and wound healing considerations are all assessed. Close coordination between the neurosurgical team and the treating oncologist ensures that surgery is timed to minimise complications while addressing urgent spinal pathology.
